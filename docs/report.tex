\begin{abstract}
\noindent
This report presents \textbf{gr-dvbs2acm}, a complete GNU Radio Out-of-Tree (OOT)
module implementing DVB-S2 Adaptive Coding and Modulation (ACM) for Low Earth Orbit
(LEO) satellite communications at X-Band (8.025~GHz), built entirely in pure Python.
The system comprises ten signal-processing blocks covering the full transmit and
receive chain, integrated with an AI/ML cognitive decision engine.

The central challenge is the LEO environment: a satellite at 500~km altitude produces
a 9-minute contact window with 12.2~dB SNR variation per pass, $\pm$188~kHz Doppler
shift, and round-trip delays as low as 3.3~ms.  Classical rule-based ACM can only
react to the \emph{current} measured SNR.  The proposed Dueling Deep Q-Network (DQN)
agent additionally exploits orbital geometry (elevation angle, pass fraction, Doppler
rate), atmospheric rain attenuation, and a 16-step SNR history to anticipate future
channel conditions and pre-emptively select the optimal Modulation-Coding scheme
(MODCOD).

Simulation results across three channel scenarios (SNR sweep, LEO pass, rain fade)
show that the converged DQN achieves 96--99\% quasi-error-free (QEF) frame rate
and 1.6--1.8~bits/symbol average spectral efficiency, compared to 47--73\% QEF
and 2.1--3.0~bits/symbol for reactive rule-based ACM, and 76--100\% QEF at a
fixed 0.99~bits/symbol for CCM.  The DQN's temporal SNR history allows proactive
MODCOD pre-positioning during fades, delivering a 56--64\% TEF gain over CCM
while maintaining near-perfect link availability on dynamic LEO and rain-fade channels.
The system is validated in a closed-loop GNU Radio Companion flowgraph
(\texttt{acm\_loopback.grc}) and a standalone Python simulation
(\texttt{acm\_loopback\_sim.py}) that requires no GNU Radio installation.

\textbf{Keywords:} DVB-S2, Adaptive Coding and Modulation, LEO satellite, GNU Radio,
Deep Q-Network, Reinforcement Learning, X-Band, LDPC, carrier recovery, Doppler.
\end{abstract}

\section{What is DVB-S2 and Why Does It Matter?}

Digital Video Broadcasting via Satellite, Second Generation (DVB-S2) is the
international standard for satellite broadband and data distribution, defined
by ETSI EN~302~307-1~\cite{etsi_dvbs2}. It is the technology used in modern
satellite internet services, scientific data downlinks, and military
communication systems.

The defining feature of DVB-S2 compared to its predecessor is \textbf{Adaptive
Coding and Modulation (ACM)}: the transmitter is allowed to change two
parameters on every single frame:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{The modulation order} --- QPSK, 8PSK, 16APSK, or 32APSK.
          Higher-order constellations carry more bits per symbol but require a
          cleaner channel.
    \item \textbf{The FEC code rate} --- from 1/4 (very high redundancy) to
          9/10 (very little redundancy). A low code rate adds more parity bits
          to protect data; a high code rate carries more information but offers
          less protection.
\end{itemize}

By varying these two parameters together (called the MODCOD, or
Modulation-Coding scheme), the system adapts the link to match the current
channel quality. In good conditions, the system uses a high-rate, high-order
MODCOD to move a lot of data. When the channel degrades (due to rain, geometry,
or interference), the system switches to a more robust MODCOD to maintain the
link without dropping to zero throughput.

\section{The Challenge: LEO Satellite Links}

This project targets \textbf{Low Earth Orbit (LEO) satellites} at 500~km
altitude operating in the X-Band (8.025~GHz). LEO links are fundamentally
different from geostationary links, and far more challenging for ACM:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Comparison of LEO vs. GEO satellite link characteristics}
\label{tab:leo_vs_geo}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
\textbf{Parameter}     & \textbf{GEO (35,786 km)}     & \textbf{LEO (500 km)} \\
\midrule
Altitude               & 35,786 km                     & 500 km \\
Pass duration          & Permanent (stationary)        & $\sim$9.2 minutes \\
Path loss (zenith)     & $\sim$196 dB                  & $\sim$164.5 dB \\
SNR swing per pass     & $\sim$1--2 dB                 & $\sim$12.2 dB \\
Doppler shift (X-Band) & $<$1 kHz (negligible)         & $\pm$188 kHz \\
RTT                    & $\sim$500 ms                  & 3.3--13.6 ms \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

The key challenges for ACM on a LEO link are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Rapid SNR variation}: The satellite rises from the horizon,
          reaches closest approach (TCA), and sets again in under 10 minutes.
          The path loss changes by 12 dB during this time, meaning the optimal
          MODCOD may span the full range from QPSK~1/4 to 32APSK~9/10 and back
          within one pass.
    \item \textbf{Large Doppler shift}: An orbital velocity of 7.62~km/s
          induces a Doppler frequency offset of up to $\pm$188~kHz at
          X-Band. This appears to the receiver as a continuous rotation of the
          IQ constellation, making demodulation impossible without compensation.
    \item \textbf{Short feedback loop}: With an RTT as low as 3.3~ms, the ACM
          feedback loop can be very fast --- but the controller must also
          predict future SNR to handle the 9-minute-long SNR arc efficiently.
\end{itemize}

\section{Why AI/ML for ACM Control?}

Classical rule-based ACM uses a fixed lookup table: if SNR $>$ threshold, use
MODCOD $m$. This works but has three weaknesses:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{It is reactive, not predictive.} Rule-based control reacts to
          the current SNR. If the channel is about to fade (e.g., satellite
          approaching horizon), the system only responds after FER rises.
    \item \textbf{The thresholds are fixed.} Different weather conditions,
          different antenna calibration states, and different interference
          patterns require different thresholds. A fixed table cannot adapt.
    \item \textbf{It cannot learn from history.} A LEO pass follows a
          repeatable orbital arc. A learning agent can recognise the SNR
          profile of a pass and pre-position the MODCOD for what is about
          to happen.
\end{enumerate}

This project replaces the threshold table with a \textbf{Deep Q-Network (DQN)}~\cite{mnih2015}
trained online during operation, augmented with an \textbf{LSTM channel
predictor}~\cite{zhang2022} that forecasts future SNR to cover the feedback loop latency.

\section{Background: Core Concepts for New Readers}
\label{sec:background}

\begin{importantnote}
If you are already comfortable with digital satellite communications and GNU Radio,
you may skip this section.  If you are new to the topic, reading it first will
make the rest of the report significantly easier to follow.
\end{importantnote}

\subsection*{Constellation Diagrams and Modulation Orders}

A \textbf{constellation diagram} is a two-dimensional map that shows how digital
bits are encoded as radio signals.  Instead of a simple on/off pulse, a modern
radio places each transmitted symbol at a specific point in a complex plane (the
I/Q plane: In-phase on the horizontal axis, Quadrature on the vertical axis).
Each point represents a unique bit pattern.

\begin{conceptbox}{Constellation analogy}
Think of it like a language alphabet.  QPSK has four ``letters'' (points), so it
can represent 2 bits per letter.  32APSK has 32 letters, so it represents 5 bits
per letter.  A larger alphabet carries more information, but the letters must be
drawn further apart so they are not confused when the paper gets smudged (noise).
\end{conceptbox}

This project uses four modulation orders:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{QPSK}: 4 constellation points --- 2 bits/symbol.  Most robust.
    \item \textbf{8PSK}: 8 points on one circle --- 3 bits/symbol.
    \item \textbf{16APSK}: 16 points on two rings --- 4 bits/symbol.
    \item \textbf{32APSK}: 32 points on three rings --- 5 bits/symbol.  Highest throughput.
\end{itemize}
Higher-order constellations pack more data but require a \emph{cleaner channel}
(higher SNR) to be decoded reliably.

\subsection*{Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)}

\textbf{SNR} (Signal-to-Noise Ratio) measures how much stronger the signal is
compared to background noise, expressed in decibels (dB).
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{SNR\,$>$\,20 dB}: Excellent.  32APSK is fully usable.
    \item \textbf{SNR\,$\approx$\,6--12 dB}: Moderate.  8PSK or 16APSK.
    \item \textbf{SNR\,$<$\,2 dB}: Poor.  Only QPSK with the lowest code rate.
    \item \textbf{SNR\,$<$\,$-2$ dB}: Near the sensitivity limit of the standard.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Forward Error Correction (FEC) and Code Rate}

\textbf{FEC} adds redundant ``check'' bits to the transmission so the receiver
can detect and correct bit errors caused by noise, without asking for a
retransmission (there is no time on a LEO pass!).

The \textbf{code rate} is the ratio of information bits to total transmitted bits:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Code rate 1/2: half the bits are check bits --- very robust but half the
          usable throughput is wasted on redundancy.
    \item Code rate 9/10: only 10\% check bits --- near-maximum efficiency, but
          only viable in a clean channel.
\end{itemize}
DVB-S2 uses two FEC layers: \textbf{LDPC} (the main error-correcting code,
operating near the Shannon limit) followed by \textbf{BCH} (an outer code that
corrects any residual errors LDPC misses)~\cite{etsi_dvbs2}.

\subsection*{MODCOD: Modulation plus Code Rate Combined}

The combination of a modulation order and a code rate is called a \textbf{MODCOD}.
DVB-S2 defines 28 MODCODs (numbered 1--28), from the most robust
(MODCOD~1: QPSK~1/4, $-2.35$~dB minimum SNR, 0.49~bits/symbol) to the most
efficient (MODCOD~28: 32APSK~9/10, $+20$~dB minimum SNR, 4.45~bits/symbol).
\textbf{ACM} means switching between these 28 MODCODs on every single frame to
always use the most efficient one the current channel can support.

\subsection*{What is GNU Radio?}

\textbf{GNU Radio} is a free, open-source framework for building radio systems in
software.  You wire together \emph{blocks} (data-processing functions) in a
flowgraph, and data flows between them as streams of numbers.  \textbf{GNU Radio
Companion (GRC)} is the graphical drag-and-drop editor for building these
flowgraphs.  An \textbf{Out-of-Tree (OOT) module} is a set of custom blocks that
extend GNU Radio with new functionality --- in this case, DVB-S2 ACM blocks.

\subsection*{Stream Tags}

GNU Radio allows small metadata labels called \textbf{stream tags} to be attached
to specific samples in the data stream.  They travel with the data through all
downstream blocks.  This project uses a \texttt{"modcod"} tag: the BB Framer
attaches the current MODCOD number to the first sample of every frame, so every
block down the chain (FEC Encoder, Modulator, Demodulator, FEC Decoder) knows
which coding and constellation to apply without a separate control channel.

\subsection*{Reinforcement Learning (RL) in One Paragraph}

\textbf{Reinforcement Learning (RL)} is a type of machine learning where an
\emph{agent} learns by trial and error.  At each step it observes the current
\emph{state} (e.g., SNR history, satellite position), takes an \emph{action}
(e.g., choose a MODCOD), and receives a \emph{reward} (e.g., throughput minus
penalty for frame errors).  Over thousands of experiences, the agent learns
which action is best in each state.  A \textbf{Deep Q-Network (DQN)} uses a
neural network to represent this mapping, allowing it to generalise across the
continuous, high-dimensional state space of a LEO satellite link.

    \item Validate the system in closed-loop simulation and prepare it for
          hardware deployment on USRP~B200.
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Related Work}
\label{ch:related_work}
% =============================================================================

This chapter surveys prior work on adaptive coding and modulation, machine
learning-based link adaptation, and LEO satellite communications, positioning
the contributions of this project relative to the state of the art.

\section{Classical ACM for Satellite Communications}

The DVB-S2 standard itself~\cite{etsi_dvbs2} introduced ACM as a per-frame
MODCOD selection mechanism driven by receiver-reported SNR.  Rinaldo and
Morello~\cite{rinaldo2004} provided the first systematic analysis of the impact
of return-channel delay on ACM performance, identifying that each additional
millisecond of feedback latency requires a larger hysteresis margin and reduces
the achievable spectral efficiency.  Gardikis et al.~\cite{gardikis2008}
demonstrated DVB-S2 ACM in a live triple-play broadband service context,
reporting throughput gains of 30--60\% over CCM under typical Ka-band weather
conditions.  The most comprehensive hardware-level validation remains the ESA
ACM demonstrator described by Bischl et al.~\cite{bischl2010}, which confirmed
the MODCOD switching thresholds of ETSI EN~302~307-1 Table~12 on a real-time
SDR platform and reported $<$0.3\,dB discrepancy between theoretical and
measured thresholds.  All of these works target GEO links with round-trip times
of approximately 500\,ms and focus on rain fade as the primary impairment;
none address the fundamentally different dynamics of a LEO pass.

\section{Machine Learning for Adaptive Modulation and Coding}

The limitation of fixed-threshold ACM motivates recent work applying supervised
and reinforcement learning to link adaptation.  Makara et al.~\cite{makara2024}
trained a deep neural network with LSTM layers to predict the optimal MODCOD
one frame ahead, achieving 91.75\% link utilisation on simulated DVB-S2 GEO
links --- an improvement over the 88.3\% achieved by the standard rule-based
controller in the same scenario.  However, their approach is supervised
(trained offline on recorded SNR traces) and does not adapt to site-specific
conditions at deployment.

Zhang et al.~\cite{zhang2022} applied a standalone LSTM to \emph{predict} the
channel SNR rather than the MODCOD directly, then fed the predicted SNR to a
conventional threshold table.  Their system achieves an optimal modulation
ratio (OMR) of 99.8\% and a mean absolute prediction error (MAE) of 0.024\,dB,
establishing a benchmark for SNR prediction accuracy.  Wang et al.~\cite{wang2019wcnc}
similarly demonstrated LSTM/GRU-based CSI prediction for adaptive modulation,
and explicitly identified reinforcement learning as the natural next step to
replace the threshold table with an end-to-end learned policy --- the approach
adopted in this work.

Chen et al.~\cite{mdpi2024snr} compared CNN and LSTM architectures for SNR
prediction in land mobile satellite scenarios, reporting spectral efficiency
improvements of $>$100\% over ACM controllers using outdated channel information,
and $>$300\% in multi-channel beam scenarios.  Rahman et al.~\cite{rahman2020}
combined fuzzy logic with differential evolution to auto-tune the ACM threshold
table, reporting a $+1.5$\,bits/s/Hz gain over fixed modulation.  None of these
works implement a full closed-loop reinforcement learning agent that learns
online from live link experience.

\section{LEO Satellite Communications and Link Adaptation}

The LEO scenario introduces qualitatively different challenges relative to GEO:
a 9-minute pass window, 12\,dB SNR swing, $\pm$188\,kHz Doppler shift, and
round-trip times as low as 3.3\,ms.  Wang et al.~\cite{wang2022sensors} defined
the \emph{Transmission Efficiency Factor} (TEF) metric that accounts for both
link availability and data rate simultaneously, reporting 796\% ACM gain over
CCM on a 500\,km LEO Ka-band link.  Their work is limited to Ka-band and does
not include an AI/ML decision layer.

Magro et al.~\cite{magro2025} recently applied DVB-S2(X) ACM to an X-Band LEO
Earth Observation satellite, the scenario most directly comparable to this work.
They demonstrated substantial throughput gains using conventional ACM but do not
incorporate any machine learning component, and their implementation targets a
dedicated flight hardware platform rather than an SDR-based GNU Radio framework.

Altunc et al.~\cite{altunc2015} validated an X-Band 8.025\,GHz downlink on a
CubeSat demonstrator, confirming the link budget assumptions (EIRP, noise figure,
antenna gain) used in our LEO channel model.

\section{GNU Radio Implementations}

Two open-source GNU Radio OOT modules provide relevant baseline functionality.
The \texttt{gr-dvbs2} module by Jarvis~\cite{gr_dvbs2} implements a complete
DVB-S2/S2X VCM/ACM transmitter in C++, including VCM stream scheduling and the
physical layer framer.  The \texttt{gr-dvbs2rx} module by Auad~\cite{gr_dvbs2rx}
implements the corresponding receiver with SIMD-accelerated LDPC decoding,
achieving real-time throughput at 10\,Msps on commodity hardware.  This project
(\texttt{gr-dvbs2acm}) extends both by providing a pure-Python implementation
with per-frame ACM switching, an integrated AI/ML cognitive engine, and a
physics-based LEO channel model --- capabilities absent from both predecessors.

\subsection{The PL Header (90 symbols)}

The PL header has two parts:

\textbf{Start of Frame (SOF) --- 26 symbols:}
A fixed, known BPSK sequence derived from the 26-bit integer
$0x18\text{D}2\text{E}82$ (binary: \texttt{01~1000~1101~0010~1110~1000~0010}).
This sequence is identical in every frame, regardless of MODCOD, frame size,
or whether pilots are enabled. It acts as a \emph{synchronisation preamble}:
the receiver continuously cross-correlates the incoming sample stream with the
known SOF pattern,
\begin{equation}
    C(\tau) = \left|\sum_{k=0}^{25} \overline{s_\text{SOF}[k]}\cdot r[\tau+k]\right|,
\end{equation}
and declares a frame boundary whenever $C(\tau)$ exceeds a detection threshold
(typically 60\,\% of the maximum possible correlation, i.e.\ $0.6\times26$).
Because the SOF is BPSK-modulated, it is readable even when the payload uses a
higher-order constellation such as 32-APSK.

\begin{plainenglish}
The receiver slides a ``template'' of the 26 known SOF symbols across the incoming
sample stream one step at a time.  At each position it computes the dot-product
between the template and the received samples.  When this dot-product is large
(above 60\% of the theoretical maximum), the receiver has found the start of a
frame.  Because the SOF is always BPSK regardless of MODCOD, this detection works
even if the receiver does not yet know which MODCOD is in use.
\end{plainenglish}

\textbf{PLSCODE --- 64 symbols:}
The 64 PLSCODE symbols encode \textbf{7 information bits} using a shortened
$(64,7)$ Reed-Muller code, giving a minimum Hamming distance of 32.  The 7
bits are arranged as:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Bits & Field & Meaning \\
\midrule
$b_6\ldots b_2$ & MODCOD & 5-bit MODCOD index (0--27, see Table~\ref{tab:kbch}) \\
$b_1$           & TYPE   & Frame size: 0 = Normal (64\,800 bits), 1 = Short (16\,200 bits) \\
$b_0$           & PILOTS & 0 = no pilots, 1 = pilot blocks inserted every 16 slots \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent
The $(64,7)$ code maps each of the $2^7=128$ possible 7-bit words to a unique
64-bit codeword with Hamming distance $\geq32$.  All 64 codeword bits are
transmitted as BPSK symbols ($0\to+1$, $1\to-1$).  The receiver takes a
soft majority decision across all 64 received real parts, which corrects up to
15 symbol errors per header --- sufficient to operate down to $E_b/N_0 \approx
-2.4$\,dB.

\subsection{\texorpdfstring{$K_\text{BCH}$}{K\_BCH}: Information Bits per Frame}
\label{sec:kbch}

\textbf{$K_\text{BCH}$} is the number of information bits that enter the BCH
encoder for one frame.  It determines the payload capacity of every PL frame.
The BCH encoder appends $t \times m$ parity bits, producing an
$N_\text{BCH}$-bit codeword that is then passed directly to the LDPC encoder
($N_\text{BCH} = K_\text{LDPC}$).

\begin{table}[H]
\centering

ETSI EN~302~307-1 Table~5a.}
\label{tab:kbch}
\begin{tabular}{llrrl}
\toprule
Code Rate & MODCOD IDs & $K_\text{BCH}$ (bits) & $N_\text{BCH}$ (bits) & BCH $t$ \\
\midrule

1/3  & 2        & 21\,408 & 21\,600 & 12 \\

\bottomrule
\end{tabular}

\noindent
The complete FEC chain for one frame is therefore:
\begin{equation}
    \underbrace{K_\text{BCH}}_{\text{payload bits}}
    \xrightarrow{\text{BCH}}
    \underbrace{N_\text{BCH} = K_\text{LDPC}}_{\text{BCH codeword}}
    \xrightarrow{\text{LDPC}}
    \underbrace{N_\text{LDPC} = 64\,800}_{\text{LDPC codeword}}
    \xrightarrow{\text{interleave + map}}
    \underbrace{N_\text{slots}\times 90}_{\text{IQ symbols}}.
\end{equation}
\begin{plainenglish}
Start with $K_\text{BCH}$ information bits (e.g.\ 32,208 for QPSK~1/2).
The BCH encoder appends a small number of parity bits, giving $N_\text{BCH}$
bits.  The LDPC encoder then pads the result up to exactly 64,800 bits by
adding many more parity bits.  Finally, the 64,800 coded bits are grouped into
symbols (2--5 bits per symbol depending on constellation) and mapped to
complex IQ points.  The output is always 64,800 bits per frame; only the
fraction that is user data changes with MODCOD.
\end{plainenglish}

\subsection{Data Slots (90 symbols each)}

After the header, the frame contains a series of 90-symbol data slots. The
number of slots depends on the MODCOD --- specifically on $K_\text{BCH}$ and
the bits-per-symbol count $B$ (2 for QPSK, 3 for 8-PSK, 4 for 16-APSK, 5 for
32-APSK):
\begin{equation}
    N_\text{slots} = \frac{N_\text{LDPC}}{B \times 90}.
\end{equation}

\noindent
For example, QPSK~1/2 (MODCOD~4) uses $B=2$, giving
$N_\text{slots} = 64\,800 / (2\times90) = 360$ data slots and a payload of
$K_\text{BCH} = 32\,208$ bits per frame.  The highest-throughput MODCOD,
32-APSK~9/10 (MODCOD~28), uses $B=5$, giving 144 slots and
$K_\text{BCH} = 58\,192$ bits per frame.

The total symbol count of a complete PL frame (with pilots) is:
\begin{equation}
    N_\text{frame} = 90 + N_\text{slots}\times 90
    + \left\lfloor\frac{N_\text{slots}-1}{16}\right\rfloor \times 36.
\end{equation}

\subsection{Pilot Blocks (36 symbols)}

Every 16 data slots, a 36-symbol pilot block is inserted. These pilot symbols
are \emph{known} BPSK symbols (all logical ones, which map to $+1+0j$ before
scrambling). After Gold-code descrambling at the receiver, they should return
to approximately $+1+0j$. Any residual phase or frequency offset rotates them
away from $+1$, and this deviation is directly measurable and correctable.

Pilots serve two critical functions in the receiver:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Phase tracking:} The receiver measures the mean phase error
    across the 36 known symbols,
    \begin{equation}
        \hat{\phi}_\text{err} = \angle\!\left(\frac{1}{36}
        \sum_{k\in\text{pilot}} r_k\right),
    \end{equation}
    and applies a first-order PLL correction $\hat{\phi} \mathrel{+}=
    \alpha_p\,\hat{\phi}_\text{err}$ (typically $\alpha_p = 0.15$).
    This keeps the carrier phase locked between successive SOF detections
    despite Doppler drift, which for a 500\,km LEO at X-band reaches
    $\dot{f}\approx\pm900$\,Hz/s.

    \item \textbf{SNR estimation (Pilot-MMSE):} Because the true symbol value
    is known, the channel gain $\hat{h}$ and noise variance
    $\hat{\sigma}^2$ can be estimated directly from each pilot block:
    \begin{equation}
        \hat{h} = \frac{1}{36}\sum_{k\in\text{pilot}} r_k,
        \qquad
        \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{36}\sum_{k\in\text{pilot}}|r_k - \hat{h}|^2,
        \qquad
        \widehat{\mathrm{SNR}} = 10\log_{10}\frac{|\hat{h}|^2}{\hat{\sigma}^2}.
    \end{equation}
    This estimate, updated every 16 data slots ($\approx 1\,440$ symbols),
    feeds the ACM controller with a high-quality, low-latency SNR measurement
    without any decision-directed feedback from the payload.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Gold Code Scrambling}

The entire PL frame (header + data + pilots) is scrambled by a complex Gold
code sequence $c_n$ before transmission. This removes spectral lines that would
occur with repetitive patterns and spreads energy evenly:
\begin{align}
    x_{n+1} &= (x_n \oplus (x_n \gg 7)) \And 0x3\text{FFFF}, \quad x_0 = 0x00001 \\
    y_{n+1} &= (y_n \oplus (y_n \gg 7) \oplus (y_n \gg 1)) \And 0x3\text{FFFF},
               \quad y_0 = 0x3\text{FFFF} \\
    g_n     &= x_n \oplus y_n \\
    c_n     &= \frac{1 - 2g_{n,1}}{\sqrt{2}} + j\frac{1 - 2g_{n,0}}{\sqrt{2}}
\end{align}
The scrambled symbol is $s'_n = s_n \cdot c_n$. Descrambling is done by
multiplying by $\overline{c_n}$ (complex conjugate), since $c_n \cdot
\overline{c_n} = 1$.

\section{Frame-Aligned Buffer Management}

DVB-S2 normal frames are 64,800 bits (LDPC codeword length). GNU Radio's
default output buffer is approximately 8,192 items --- eight times smaller than
one frame. If a block tries to write a complete frame but only 8,192 slots are
available, the write would overflow.

The official GNU Radio solution for this class of problem is
\texttt{set\_output\_multiple(N)}: the block calls this once in its
\texttt{\_\_init\_\_}, and the scheduler guarantees that it will never call
\texttt{general\_work} unless at least $N$ output slots are free. The block can
then write a complete frame in one call with no risk of overflow:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=python, caption={Frame-aligned buffer pattern using set\_output\_multiple}]
def __init__(self, ...):
    gr.basic_block.__init__(self, ...)
    # Guarantee >= N output slots before general_work is called.
    self.set_output_multiple(N)

def general_work(self, input_items, output_items):
    out0 = output_items[0]
    # len(out0) is guaranteed >= N — write a full frame safely
    frame = self._compute_frame(...)
    out0[:len(frame)] = frame
    return len(frame)
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Transmit Chain Blocks}

\subsection{BB Framer ACM (\texttt{bb\_framer\_acm\_py.py})}

The Baseband Framer is the first block in the transmit chain. It takes raw
information bits and wraps them in a \textbf{BBFRAME} --- a standardised
container defined in ETSI EN~302~307-1 Annex C.

\textbf{What it does:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Reads the current MODCOD from a message port connected to the ACM
          Controller.
    \item Looks up the maximum data field length (DFL) for that MODCOD.
    \item Reads exactly DFL bits from the input stream.
    \item Constructs a 10-byte BBHEADER containing:
          \begin{itemize}[nosep]
              \item \textbf{MATYPE-1}: Frame type flags (TS/GS, SIS/MIS, CCM/ACM, roll-off)
              \item \textbf{MATYPE-2}: Encodes the MODCOD ID (ACM mode)
              \item \textbf{UPL}: User Packet Length (188 bytes for MPEG-TS)
              \item \textbf{DFL}: Data Field Length (the number of user data bits)
              \item \textbf{SYNC}: 0x47 (standard MPEG-TS sync byte)
              \item \textbf{SYNCD}: Position of the first complete user packet
              \item \textbf{CRC-8}: ITU-T CRC of all preceding header bytes
          \end{itemize}
    \item Outputs the BBHEADER followed by the DFL data bits.
    \item Attaches \texttt{"modcod"}, \texttt{"frame\_start"}, and
          \texttt{"pilots"} stream tags at the first sample.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{FEC Encoder ACM (\texttt{fec\_encoder\_acm\_py.py})}

The FEC encoder applies the two-stage DVB-S2 error correction coding defined
in ETSI EN~302~307-1 \S5:

\textbf{Stage 1 --- BCH outer code:} Takes $k_\text{BCH}$ information bits and
computes BCH parity bits using the DVB-S2 generator polynomial $g(x)$ for the
given code rate. The result is a BCH codeword of $n_\text{BCH}$ bits. The BCH
code provides error correction for up to $t = 8$ or $t = 12$ bit errors (rate
dependent) after LDPC decoding.

\textbf{Stage 2 --- LDPC inner code:} Encodes the BCH codeword to produce the
final 64,800-bit LDPC codeword. The LDPC encoder uses the exact sparse
parity-check matrix $H$ defined by the standard, implemented via the
\texttt{gr-dtv} module's \texttt{dvb\_bch\_bb} and \texttt{dvb\_ldpc\_bb}
blocks. A separate sub-flowgraph is maintained for each of the 28 MODCODs.

The parity computation for the LDPC code uses a structured accumulator:
\begin{equation}
    p_j = \bigoplus_{i \in \mathcal{N}(j)} b_i
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{N}(j)$ is the set of information bit indices that are connected
to parity bit $j$ according to the DVB-S2 parity check matrix.

\subsection{Modulator ACM (\texttt{modulator\_acm\_py.py})}

The modulator maps the 64,800 encoded bits to complex IQ symbols according to
the ETSI-specified constellation for the current MODCOD:

\textbf{QPSK (MODCODs 1--11):} Four points at $(\pm 1 \pm j)/\sqrt{2}$.
Gray-coded: 2 bits per symbol.

\textbf{8PSK (MODCODs 12--17):} Eight points uniformly on the unit circle:
$e^{j2\pi k/8}$ for $k=0,\ldots,7$, with DVB-S2 specific bit labelling
$\{1,0,3,2,5,4,7,6\}$. 3 bits per symbol.

\textbf{16APSK (MODCODs 18--23):} Two concentric rings with 4 and 12 points.
Inner ring radius $r_1 \approx 0.6$, outer ring $r_2 = 1.0$ (ratio
$\gamma_1 = r_2/r_1$ is code-rate dependent). 4 bits per symbol.

\textbf{32APSK (MODCODs 24--28):} Three concentric rings with 4, 12, and 16
points. Ratios $\gamma_1 = r_2/r_1$, $\gamma_2 = r_3/r_1$ are code-rate
dependent. 5 bits per symbol.

The bit-to-symbol mapping is:
\begin{equation}
    \text{idx} = \sum_{k=0}^{B-1} b_k \cdot 2^{B-1-k},
    \qquad s = \mathcal{C}[\text{idx}]
\end{equation}
where $B$ is the number of bits per symbol and $\mathcal{C}$ is the
constellation array.

\textbf{Frame-boundary deferred MODCOD switching.}
A subtle but critical implementation detail is that the modulator must not apply
a new MODCOD constellation immediately upon receiving a \texttt{modcod} stream
tag. The internal buffer \texttt{\_in\_buf} may still contain bits that were
encoded for the \emph{previous} MODCOD frame. Applying a different constellation
mapping to those bits would scatter them across wrong symbol positions (e.g.\
QPSK-encoded bits mapped as 16APSK produce 16 spurious scatter points). The
implementation stores the incoming tag in \texttt{\_pending\_modcod} and applies
the switch only at the next frame boundary, once a complete 64\,800-bit LDPC
codeword from the new MODCOD has arrived. This guarantees that every set of
64\,800 bits is mapped using exactly one consistent constellation, producing
clean 4-, 8-, 16-, or 32-point clusters on the constellation sink.

\subsection{PL Framer ACM (\texttt{pl\_framer\_acm\_py.py})}

The PL Framer takes the IQ symbols from the modulator and constructs the
complete Physical Layer frame as specified in ETSI \S11.3:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Build the SOF (26 symbols):} Convert the 26-bit integer
          $0x18D2E82$ to BPSK symbols: bit 0 maps to $+1/\sqrt{2}$,
          bit 1 maps to $-1/\sqrt{2}$.
    \item \textbf{Build the PLSCODE (64 symbols):} Construct the 8-bit code
          word \texttt{modcod[4:0] || pilots || 0 || 0}, repeat it 8 times to
          get 64 bits, then BPSK-modulate.
    \item \textbf{Insert pilot blocks:} Every 16 data slots (1440 symbols),
          insert a 36-symbol block of all-ones BPSK ($+1/\sqrt{2}$ repeated
          36 times).
    \item \textbf{Apply Gold code scrambling:} Multiply every symbol by the
          corresponding $c_n$ from the Gold sequence generator.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Receive Chain Blocks}

\subsection{PL Sync ACM (\texttt{pl\_sync\_acm\_py.py}) --- Carrier Recovery and Frame Synchronisation}
\label{sec:pl_sync}

The PL Synchroniser is the most complex receiver block. It must solve three
coupled problems simultaneously: find where each frame starts, compensate for
the Doppler frequency offset (which rotates the constellation continuously),
and remove the Gold code scrambling. These are described in detail below.

\subsubsection{Problem 1: Spinning Constellation (Frequency Offset)}

A Doppler frequency offset of $f_d$ Hz causes each received IQ sample to be
rotated by an extra angle of $2\pi f_d / f_s$ radians (where $f_s$ is the
sample rate). Because this rotation accumulates with time, the constellation
appears to \emph{spin} rather than sitting at fixed points. A frequency offset
of even 1~Hz at $f_s = 1$~Msps would rotate the constellation by 6.28 radians
per second. At the maximum LEO Doppler of 188~kHz, the constellation makes
$188,000$ full rotations per second --- completely unrecoverable without
correction.

The solution is to apply an equal and opposite rotation to every received
sample:
\begin{equation}
    r_\text{corrected}[k] = r[k] \cdot e^{-j(\hat{\phi} + \hat{f} \cdot k)}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{f}$ is the estimated frequency offset in radians/sample and
$\hat{\phi}$ is the accumulated phase offset.

\begin{plainenglish}
If the constellation is spinning clockwise due to Doppler, we spin it
anti-clockwise by the same amount at each sample.  The term $e^{-j(\hat{\phi}
+ \hat{f} \cdot k)}$ is a unit-magnitude complex number (a ``rotation wheel'')
whose angle increases by $\hat{f}$ radians with each new sample.  Multiplying
the received sample by this counter-rotation cancels the Doppler spin and
freezes the constellation at fixed points.
\end{plainenglish} This is implemented as:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=python, caption={Per-sample frequency and phase correction}]
def _apply_correction(self, samples):
    n = len(samples)
    k = np.arange(n, dtype=np.float64)
    # Phase at each sample: accumulated + ramp from freq offset
    phase_vec  = self._phase_acc + self._freq_est * k
    correction = np.exp(-1j * phase_vec).astype(np.complex64)
    # Advance the accumulator for the next call
    self._phase_acc += self._freq_est * n
    self._phase_acc  = (self._phase_acc + np.pi) % (2*np.pi) - np.pi
    return samples * correction
\end{lstlisting}

\subsubsection{Problem 2: Frame Boundary Detection (SOF Correlation)}

To process frames, the receiver must first find where they start. The SOF is
the only known fixed sequence in every frame, so the receiver slides a
26-sample correlation window across the incoming stream and looks for a peak:
\begin{equation}
    C(\tau) = \left| \sum_{k=0}^{25} \overline{s_\text{SOF}[k]} \cdot r[\tau+k] \right|
\end{equation}
A frame start is declared when $C(\tau) > \theta_\text{SOF}$, where
$\theta_\text{SOF} = 0.6 \times 26$ (60\% of maximum possible correlation).

Crucially, the correlation is \emph{complex-valued}: its phase
$\angle C(\tau_\text{peak})$ directly gives the residual carrier phase at the
SOF location. Successive SOF phases, divided by the time between them,
give the frequency offset:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{f}_\text{new} = \frac{(\phi_\text{SOF,now} - \phi_\text{SOF,prev})
    \bmod [-\pi, \pi)}{\Delta t_\text{samples}}
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \hat{f} \leftarrow \hat{f} + \alpha_f \hat{f}_\text{new}
    \quad (\alpha_f = 0.05, \text{slow loop})
\end{equation}
The slow loop gain prevents over-correction from a noisy single measurement.

\subsubsection{Problem 3: Pilot-Aided Phase Tracking}

Even after frequency correction, a residual slowly-varying phase offset remains
due to oscillator phase noise and imperfect frequency estimation. DVB-S2 pilot
blocks solve this: after Gold descrambling, pilot symbols should be $+1+0j$.
Any deviation is a direct measurement of residual phase error:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\phi}_\text{err} = \angle\left(\frac{1}{36}\sum_{k \in \text{pilot}} r_k\right)
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\phi} \leftarrow \hat{\phi} + \alpha_p \hat{\phi}_\text{err}
    \quad (\alpha_p = 0.15, \text{pilot PLL gain})
\end{equation}
This is applied every time a pilot block is found in the descrambled data,
giving a phase correction every $16 \times 90 + 36 = 1476$ symbols.

\subsubsection{M{\"u}ller \& Mueller Timing Error Detector}

Timing synchronisation estimates fractional symbol timing offset. The Müller
\& Mueller (M\&M) decision-directed timing error detector operates at 1 sample
per symbol (unlike Gardner TED which requires $\geq 2$):
\begin{equation}
    e[k] = \mathrm{Re}\!\left\{ d[k] \cdot \overline{x[k-1]} - d[k-1] \cdot \overline{x[k]} \right\}
\end{equation}
where $x[k]$ is the current received sample and $d[k]$ is the nearest
constellation point (hard decision). The error $e[k]$ is averaged over each
frame and reported as a diagnostic metric. At 1 sample/symbol it cannot drive
a resampler directly, but provides a timing quality indicator. Future work
would extend this to $\geq 2$ sps with a polyphase filterbank resampler.

\subsubsection{PLSCODE Decoding}

After frame acquisition and carrier correction, the 64 PLSCODE symbols are
decoded by a majority-vote Reed-Muller decoder: each of the 8 information bits
is decoded from its 8 repetitions, requiring at least 4 of 8 copies to agree:
\begin{equation}
    b_i = \begin{cases} 1 & \sum_{k=0}^{7} \hat{b}_{i+8k} \geq 4 \\ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}
\end{equation}
The 8-bit word yields the MODCOD ID (bits 7--3) and the pilots flag (bit 2).

\subsection{SNR Estimator (\texttt{snr\_estimator\_py.py})}

The SNR estimator is responsible for measuring the signal quality and providing
the ACM controller with an accurate, low-latency SNR estimate. Three
complementary algorithms are fused via a Kalman filter.

\textbf{Pilot-MMSE Estimator} (used when pilots are present):
When pilot symbols are present, the receiver knows the true transmitted symbol
($s_p = 1+0j$ after descrambling). The channel gain $h$ is estimated from
pilot symbols, and the noise variance from the residual:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{h} = \frac{1}{N_p}\sum_{n \in \text{pilots}} r_n, \qquad
    \hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{N_p}\sum_{n \in \text{pilots}} |r_n - \hat{h}|^2
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{\text{SNR}}_\text{pilot} = 10\log_{10}\frac{|\hat{h}|^2}{\hat{\sigma}^2}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Blind M2M4 Estimator}~\cite{pauluzzi2000} (works without pilot knowledge):
Uses the second and fourth moments of the received signal, which have a known
mathematical relationship to SNR for all standard QAM/PSK constellations:
\begin{align}
    \hat{P}   &= \mathbb{E}[|r|^2], \qquad \hat{M}_4 = \mathbb{E}[|r|^4] \\
    \hat{\sigma}^2 &\approx \sqrt{2\hat{P}^2 - \hat{M}_4}, \qquad
    \widehat{\text{SNR}}_\text{M2M4} = \frac{\hat{P} - \hat{\sigma}^2}{\hat{\sigma}^2}
\end{align}

\textbf{Kalman Filter Fusion:}
The two estimates are fused in a scalar Kalman filter, treating the SNR as
a slowly-varying state variable:
\begin{align}
    \hat{x}^-_k &= \hat{x}_{k-1} \quad \text{(predict: constant-SNR model)} \\
    K_k &= \frac{P^-_k}{P^-_k + R} \quad \text{(Kalman gain)} \\
    \hat{x}_k &= \hat{x}^-_k + K_k(z_k - \hat{x}^-_k) \quad \text{(update)}
\end{align}
where $z_k$ is the pilot-MMSE estimate (if pilots present) or M2M4 estimate
otherwise. The Kalman filter reduces measurement noise and provides a smooth
estimate suitable for ACM control decisions.

\begin{plainenglish}
The Kalman filter works like a ``weighted average with memory.''  The predict
step says ``I expect the SNR to be roughly the same as it was last time.''  The
update step then blends that prediction with the new noisy measurement, giving
more weight to the prediction when the new measurement is uncertain (high $R$),
and more weight to the new measurement when it is reliable.  Over time, the
filter output is a smooth, noise-reduced SNR track rather than a noisy
measurement-by-measurement estimate.
\end{plainenglish}

\subsection{Demodulator ACM (\texttt{demodulator\_acm\_py.py})}

The demodulator converts the received IQ samples to \textbf{Log-Likelihood
Ratios (LLRs)} for soft-decision FEC decoding. An LLR is a real number that
represents how confident the receiver is about each bit:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Large positive LLR $\Rightarrow$ bit is very likely 0
    \item Large negative LLR $\Rightarrow$ bit is very likely 1
    \item LLR near zero $\Rightarrow$ uncertain
\end{itemize}

The max-log approximation to the optimal LLR is:
\begin{equation}
    L(b_k\,|\,y) \approx \frac{1}{2\hat{\sigma}^2}
    \left[\min_{s \in \mathcal{S}_k^{(0)}} |y-s|^2 -
          \min_{s \in \mathcal{S}_k^{(1)}} |y-s|^2\right]
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{S}_k^{(0)}$ and $\mathcal{S}_k^{(1)}$ are the constellation
points with bit $k$ equal to 0 and 1, respectively, and $\hat{\sigma}^2$ is
the noise variance from the SNR estimator. Soft LLRs give approximately
2~dB better LDPC decoding performance compared to hard-bit decisions.

\begin{plainenglish}
For each bit position $k$ in the received symbol, the LLR answers the question:
``How much closer is the received point $y$ to a constellation point where this
bit is 0 versus a constellation point where this bit is 1?''

If $y$ is much closer to the ``bit-0'' points, the LLR is large and positive
(confident: bit is 0).  If $y$ is much closer to the ``bit-1'' points, the LLR
is large and negative (confident: bit is 1).  If $y$ is equally far from both
groups, the LLR is near zero (uncertain: noise has obliterated this bit).

Passing LLRs (soft decisions) rather than hard 0/1 bits to the LDPC decoder
gives the decoder confidence information --- it knows which bits are reliable
anchors and which are uncertain, allowing it to recover from errors much more
effectively.
\end{plainenglish}

The demodulator reads the \texttt{"modcod"} stream tag to know which
constellation to use for each frame. It then deinterleaves the LLRs back to
natural bit order (reversing the DVB-S2 bit-interleaver applied at the encoder).

\subsection{FEC Decoder ACM (\texttt{fec\_decoder\_acm\_py.py})}
\label{sec:fec_decoder}

The FEC decoder is the computational heart of the receiver. It performs the
inverse of the FEC encoder: recover the original $k_\text{BCH}$ information
bits from the noisy 64,800 LLRs. The decoder uses a two-stage approach:
LDPC inner decoding followed by BCH outer correction.

\subsubsection{Stage 1: Encoder-Guided LDPC Decoder (Min-Sum Bit-Flipping)}

The LDPC code is a sparse linear code with a 64,800-bit codeword and a
structured parity-check matrix $H$. A valid codeword satisfies $H\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{0}$
over GF(2). The receiver's task is to find the most likely codeword consistent
with the noisy observations (the LLRs).

Full belief propagation (message-passing) LDPC decoding requires storing and
iterating the sparse $H$ matrix, which is complex to implement from scratch.
This implementation takes a simpler but effective approach: it uses the
\textbf{gr-dtv LDPC encoder as an oracle} for the $H$ matrix.

The algorithm works as follows:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Initial estimate}: Hard-decide the LLRs to get an initial
          codeword estimate: $\hat{b}_n = \mathbb{1}[L_n > 0]$.
    \item \textbf{Re-encode}: Feed the first $k_\text{BCH}$ bits through the
          gr-dtv DVB-S2 LDPC encoder to get a complete 64,800-bit codeword.
    \item \textbf{Syndrome check}: Compare the re-encoded codeword to the
          estimated bits. Any mismatch is a ``syndrome-failing'' position where
          the current estimate is probably wrong.
    \item \textbf{Flip weakest bits}: Among all syndrome-failing positions,
          flip the 25\% of bits that have the smallest $|L_n|$ (least
          reliable). These are the bits most likely to have been incorrectly
          hard-decided.
    \item \textbf{Repeat} steps 2--4 for up to 3 iterations, or until the
          syndrome is zero (perfect codeword found).
\end{enumerate}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=python, caption={Encoder-guided LDPC bit-flipping decoder (core loop)}]
for _ in range(max_iter):
    codeword = sg.encode(bits[:kbch])    # gr-dtv: exact DVB-S2 H matrix
    syndrome  = (bits[:nldpc] != codeword) # True where bits disagree
    n_errors  = int(syndrome.sum())

    if n_errors == 0:
        return bits[:kbch], True, 0      # Converged

    # Flip the least-reliable bits in failing positions
    syn_pos = np.where(syndrome)[0]
    n_flip  = max(1, int(len(syn_pos) * 0.25))
    weakest = syn_pos[np.argsort(llr_abs[syn_pos])[:n_flip]]
    bits[weakest] ^= 1                   # Flip
\end{lstlisting}

This approach delivers approximately 0.5--0.8~dB coding gain over a pure
hard-decision decoder. The iteration count is capped at 3 because each
iteration requires one gr-dtv encoder call (approximately 20--50~ms per
64,800-bit frame).

\subsubsection{Stage 2: BCH Outer Decoder}

After LDPC decoding, the recovered $k_\text{BCH}$ bits may still contain a
small number of residual errors. The BCH outer code is designed to correct
these. Verification is done by re-encoding:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Feed the $k_\text{BCH}$ recovered bits through the gr-dtv BCH
          encoder. If the result matches the input exactly, the BCH code is
          satisfied and the bits are correct.
    \item If not, attempt \textbf{single-bit correction}: find the bit with
          the smallest $|L_n|$ (least confident), flip it, re-encode, and
          check again. If this fixes the BCH syndrome, accept the correction.
    \item If single-bit correction also fails, output the bits as-is and
          flag the frame as a BCH error.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{ACM Controller (\texttt{acm\_controller\_py.py})}
\label{sec:acm_controller}

The ACM controller is the decision engine of the feedback loop. It receives
consolidated link quality reports and selects the MODCOD to be used for the
next frame. Two modes are implemented:

\textbf{Rule-based mode} (default): A two-parameter asymmetric hysteresis
controller with margin $\delta = 1.0$~dB and hysteresis half-gap $h = 0.5$~dB,
applied to the MODCOD decision threshold (\texttt{threshold\_db} $=$ \texttt{min\_snr\_db} $+ 0.5$~dB):
\begin{align}
    \text{upgrade if:}& \quad \hat{\text{SNR}} \geq \text{threshold}(m^*) + \delta + h = \text{SNR}_\text{min}(m^*) + 2.0~\text{dB} \label{eq:upgrade}\\
    \text{downgrade if:}& \quad \hat{\text{SNR}} < \text{threshold}(m_\text{cur}) + \delta - h = \text{SNR}_\text{min}(m_\text{cur}) + 1.0~\text{dB} \label{eq:downgrade}
\end{align}
This creates a 1.0~dB hysteresis deadband between the downgrade boundary
($\text{SNR}_\text{min} + 1.0$~dB) and the upgrade boundary ($\text{SNR}_\text{min} + 2.0$~dB),
preventing ping-pong switching near a threshold.  The SNR estimate
$\hat{\text{SNR}}$ is exponentially smoothed with $\alpha = 0.1$ before the
decision is evaluated, further suppressing transient fading dips.

A MODCOD change is only published when the selected $m^*$ differs from the
currently active MODCOD. The controller also tracks the full channel state
received from the LEO channel model: elevation angle, pass fraction, slant
range, Doppler shift, RTT, FSPL, rain, gas, cloud, scintillation, and Rician
fading values. Console output is printed \emph{only} on a MODCOD switch event,
showing the old and new MODCOD, smoothed SNR, elevation, Doppler, and RTT in a
single line:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=bash, caption={MODCOD switch console output format (UP/DOWN arrows shown as ASCII)}]
[GRC-ACM] ^ QPSK 1/2 -> 32APSK 9/10 | SNR=+18.8 dB  el=82.5  Doppler=+12.1 kHz  RTT=3.4 ms
[GRC-ACM] v 32APSK 9/10 -> 8PSK 2/3  | SNR=+8.3 dB   el=6.1   Doppler=-145.2 kHz RTT=12.8 ms
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{AI mode}: The decision is delegated to the DQN agent described in
Chapter~\ref{ch:ai}. The controller sends the state vector to the agent via
ZMQ and waits for a MODCOD action.

\section{Motivation: Why Reinforcement Learning?}

\subsection{The Limitation of Rule-Based ACM}

Classical ACM uses a lookup table of SNR thresholds. When the measured SNR
crosses a threshold, the controller switches MODCOD. This approach is simple
and deterministic but fundamentally \emph{reactive}: it can only respond to
what has already happened. Consider a satellite approaching the horizon during
a rain event: the SNR is falling at several dB per minute and rain attenuation
is increasing simultaneously. A rule-based controller keeps the aggressive
high-rate MODCOD right up until frame errors accumulate --- only after observing
errors does it switch to a safer MODCOD, meaning frames are already being lost.

Furthermore, a rule-based controller treats every situation with SNR = 10\,dB
identically, regardless of whether the channel is stable, rapidly fading, or
recovering from a deep fade. It cannot use the \emph{trajectory} of recent
SNR measurements to anticipate what is about to happen.

A learning-based controller can instead:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Observe that SNR is falling at 2\,dB/s and pre-emptively switch
          down \emph{before} frames start failing.
    \item Recognise the characteristic AOS (Acquisition of Signal) SNR ramp
          and aggressively upshift to exploit the improving channel.
    \item Use orbital geometry information (elevation, pass fraction) that is
          entirely unknown to the threshold table.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Formulating ACM as a Markov Decision Process}

A Markov Decision Process (MDP) is the standard framework for sequential
decision-making under uncertainty. It has four components:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{State} $s_t \in \mathbb{R}^{52}$: Everything the agent
          observes at time step $t$ --- SNR history, orbital geometry, BER,
          FER, active MODCOD (detailed in Section~\ref{sec:state}).
    \item \textbf{Action} $a_t \in \{1, \ldots, 28\}$: The MODCOD index
          selected for the next frame.
    \item \textbf{Reward} $r_t \in \mathbb{R}$: Scalar feedback encoding
          the quality of the decision: spectral efficiency subject to QoS
          and stability constraints (Section~\ref{sec:reward}).
    \item \textbf{Transition} $P(s_{t+1} \mid s_t, a_t)$: How the satellite
          channel evolves. This is \emph{unknown} to the agent --- it depends
          on orbital geometry, weather, hardware impairments, and interference.
\end{itemize}

The agent's goal is to find the optimal policy $\pi^*$ --- a mapping from
states to actions --- that maximises the expected discounted sum of future
rewards:
\begin{equation}
    \pi^* = \arg\max_\pi\; \mathbb{E}\!\left[\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k r_{t+k}\right]
\end{equation}
The discount factor $\gamma = 0.95$ balances immediate link quality against
long-term throughput: a reward 20 steps ahead is worth $0.95^{20} \approx
0.36$ of its immediate value, encouraging the agent to think several seconds
ahead without over-weighting speculative distant futures.

\begin{plainenglish}
The agent does not just try to maximise the \emph{current} throughput ---
it tries to maximise the \emph{total} throughput over the whole pass.
The discount factor $\gamma$ is like saying: ``A reward in 2 seconds is worth
0.95 times a reward right now; a reward in 4 seconds is worth $0.95^2 \approx 0.9$
times; and so on.''  Setting $\gamma = 0.95$ makes the agent care about the next
$\sim$20 steps (roughly 2 seconds at 10~Hz) --- long enough to anticipate a
satellite horizon fade, but not so far ahead that uncertainty makes planning useless.
\end{plainenglish}

\subsection{Why Deep Q-Learning?}

Q-learning learns the \emph{value} of taking each action in each state,
without needing an explicit model of the transition dynamics. The Q-value
(action-value function) is defined as the expected total discounted reward:
\begin{equation}
    Q^\pi(s, a) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k r_{t+k}
    \;\Big|\; s_t = s,\; a_t = a,\; \pi\right]
\end{equation}
The optimal Q-function satisfies the Bellman optimality equation:
\begin{equation}
    Q^*(s, a) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[r + \gamma \max_{a'} Q^*(s', a')\right]
\end{equation}
Once $Q^*$ is known, the optimal policy is $\pi^*(s) = \arg\max_a Q^*(s,a)$
--- a greedy lookup, $O(1)$ per decision.

With a 52-dimensional continuous state space and 28 discrete actions, a
tabular Q-table is infeasible. A \textbf{Deep Q-Network (DQN)}~\cite{mnih2015}
replaces it with a neural network $Q_\theta(s, a)$ trained by gradient descent
to satisfy the Bellman equation over a large set of observed transitions.

\begin{conceptbox}{What is a Neural Network? (Primer for New Readers)}
A neural network is simply a chain of linear transformations with non-linear
activations in between.  One layer computes:
\[
  \mathbf{y} = \mathrm{ReLU}(\mathbf{W}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b})
\]
where $\mathbf{x}$ is the input vector, $\mathbf{W}$ is a matrix of
\emph{learnable weights}, $\mathbf{b}$ is a bias vector, and
$\mathrm{ReLU}(z) = \max(0,z)$ clips negatives to zero.  Stacking three
such layers gives the DQN used here:

\smallskip
\centerline{Input ($\mathbb{R}^{52}$) $\xrightarrow{\mathbf{W}_1,\mathbf{b}_1}$
Layer 1 ($\mathbb{R}^{256}$) $\xrightarrow{\mathbf{W}_2,\mathbf{b}_2}$
Layer 2 ($\mathbb{R}^{256}$) $\xrightarrow{}$ Q-values ($\mathbb{R}^{28}$)}
\smallskip

\textbf{Training} = finding weights that make the output useful.  The
procedure is always the same four steps:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Forward pass:} feed in the 52-dim state, get 28 Q-values out.
  \item \textbf{Loss:} compute how wrong the Q-values are compared to the
        Bellman target $y_t$ (a single scalar error number).
  \item \textbf{Backward pass (backpropagation):} compute the gradient
        $\nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}$ --- how much each weight contributed to the error.
  \item \textbf{Weight update:} nudge every weight in the direction that
        reduces the error: $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta\,\nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}$,
        where $\eta = 3\times10^{-4}$ is the learning rate (step size).
\end{enumerate}
After thousands of such updates the weights stabilise to values that
correctly predict long-term ACM throughput --- the network has ``learned''
to evaluate MODCOD choices.
\end{conceptbox}

\begin{conceptbox}{What is Epsilon-Greedy Exploration?}
At the start of training the agent knows nothing useful, so it must
\emph{explore} --- try random actions to discover which are good.
Over time it shifts to \emph{exploiting} what it has learned.

The $\varepsilon$-greedy rule balances this:
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item With probability $\varepsilon$: pick a random MODCOD (\textbf{explore}).
  \item With probability $1-\varepsilon$: pick the MODCOD with the highest
        Q-value (\textbf{exploit}).
\end{itemize}
$\varepsilon$ starts at 1.0 (pure exploration) and decays by
$\times 0.99997$ per step, reaching 0.05 after $\approx$100,000 steps.
At that point the agent picks randomly only 5\% of the time --- enough to
continue discovering rare edge conditions while mostly exploiting its
learned policy.

In this project, exploration is additionally \emph{guided}: only MODCODs
within 2\,dB of the current SNR are eligible for random selection.
This prevents wasting experience on obviously unreachable MODCODs
(e.g.\ 32APSK~9/10 when SNR = $-1$\,dB).
\end{conceptbox}

\section{System Architecture}

\subsection{Process Isolation via ZMQ}

The AI engine runs in a separate Python process connected to the GNU Radio
ACM Controller block via a ZeroMQ (ZMQ) message socket. This isolation is
essential for three reasons:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Non-blocking real-time operation:} GNU Radio's scheduler
          requires deterministic sample timing. Neural network inference
          ($\sim$1\,ms per decision) must not block signal-processing threads.
          ZMQ allows the C++ controller to query the AI engine asynchronously.
    \item \textbf{Independent training:} The DQN and CNN+LSTM predictor train
          in a background thread at 10\,Hz without contending for the
          signal-processing CPU budget.
    \item \textbf{Restart-ability:} The AI engine can be restarted, upgraded,
          or replaced without stopping the GNU Radio flowgraph.
\end{enumerate}

Communication uses \textbf{ZMQ REQ/REP} on \texttt{tcp://127.0.0.1:5557}.
The C++ controller sends a JSON state dictionary and blocks on the reply.
The protocol carries the full 52-dimensional state including all five
channel-state fields from the LEO channel model:

\subsection{Threading Model}

The AI engine maintains two threads:

\textbf{Main thread (ZMQ server):} Receives state queries from GNU Radio,
runs a DQN forward pass ($\sim$1\,ms on CPU), and returns the selected
MODCOD with its Q-gap confidence score. This thread has strict latency
requirements.

\textbf{Training thread (10\,Hz):} Every 100\,ms, draws a PER-weighted
minibatch from the replay buffer, computes the Double DQN Bellman loss,
performs one Adam gradient step with cosine LR scheduling, and updates
the CNN+LSTM predictor on recent SNR history. The target network is
soft-updated after every 200 training steps using Polyak averaging.

\section{State Representation}
\label{sec:state}

MODCOD decision. A \textbf{52-dimensional vector} is used, combining SNR
history, five LEO orbital geometry features from the channel model, the
current MODCOD one-hot encoding, and three scalar link quality metrics:

\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{s}_t = \bigl[
        \underbrace{\overline{\text{SNR}}_{t-15},\ldots,\overline{\text{SNR}}_t}_{16},\;

        \underbrace{\log\text{BER}_t,\;\log\text{FER}_t,\;\Delta\overline{\text{SNR}}_t}_{3}
    \bigr] \in \mathbb{R}^{52}
\end{equation}

\section{Dueling Deep Q-Network Architecture}

\subsection{Q-Value Function and Optimal Policy}

The Q-network $Q_\theta(s, a)$ takes the 52-dimensional state vector and
outputs a 28-dimensional vector of Q-values, one per MODCOD:
\begin{equation}
    \pi^*(s) = \arg\max_{a \in \{1,\ldots,28\}} Q_\theta(s, a)
\end{equation}

\subsection{Dueling Architecture}

A standard Q-network maps $(s, a) \to Q(s,a)$ as a monolithic function.
The \textbf{Dueling DQN}~\cite{wang2016dueling} decomposes the Q-value into two
interpretable streams:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Value stream} $V(s)$: How good is state $s$ regardless of
          which MODCOD is chosen? Near the horizon at low SNR, no MODCOD
          gives a good outcome --- $V(s)$ captures this context.
    \item \textbf{Advantage stream} $A(s, a)$: How much better is MODCOD $a$
          relative to the average MODCOD in this state?
\end{itemize}
The Q-value is reconstructed as:
\begin{equation}
    Q(s, a;\,\theta) = V(s;\,\theta_V) + \left[
        A(s, a;\,\theta_A) - \frac{1}{28}\sum_{a'} A(s, a';\,\theta_A)
    \right]
\end{equation}
Subtracting the mean advantage removes the identifiability ambiguity
(otherwise $V$ and $A$ could compensate each other arbitrarily). The benefit
for ACM is that near SNR threshold boundaries, where many MODCODs have
similar Q-values, the separation of $V$ and $A$ yields more stable MODCOD
rankings because $V(s)$ is learned from all 28 actions simultaneously.

\begin{plainenglish}
\textbf{Value} $V(s)$: How good is the situation overall?  If the satellite is
near the horizon in heavy rain, the situation is bad regardless of MODCOD choice
--- $V(s)$ will be low.  If the satellite is overhead at high SNR, $V(s)$ is high.

\textbf{Advantage} $A(s,a)$: Given the situation, how much better is this specific
MODCOD compared to the average MODCOD?  A large positive $A$ for MODCOD 14 means
``MODCOD 14 is distinctly better than the others right now.''

Splitting these two concepts into separate streams makes learning faster and more
stable: the network learns ``how good is the situation'' independently of ``which
MODCOD is best.''  This is especially valuable near SNR threshold boundaries where
the differences between adjacent MODCODs are small.
\end{plainenglish}

\begin{lstlisting}[language=python, caption={Dueling DQN architecture (PyTorch)}]
class DuelingDQNNetwork(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, state_dim=52, n_actions=28, hidden_dim=256):
        super().__init__()
        # Shared feature extractor with LayerNorm for small-batch stability
        self.feature = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(state_dim, hidden_dim), nn.LayerNorm(hidden_dim),
            nn.ReLU(), nn.Dropout(0.1),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, hidden_dim), nn.LayerNorm(hidden_dim),
            nn.ReLU(),
        )
        # Value stream V(s) -> scalar
        self.value_stream = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, hidden_dim // 2), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim // 2, 1)
        )
        # Advantage stream A(s,a) -> 28 values
        self.advantage_stream = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, hidden_dim // 2), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim // 2, n_actions)
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        f = self.feature(x)
        V = self.value_stream(f)
        A = self.advantage_stream(f)
        return V + A - A.mean(dim=1, keepdim=True)   # Q = V + (A - mean(A))
\end{lstlisting}

\textbf{LayerNorm vs BatchNorm:} Batch Normalisation requires large batches
for stable statistics. During online satellite operation, the batch size is
limited to 64 transitions, and early in training the buffer may have fewer.
LayerNorm normalises across the feature dimension (independent of batch size)
and is therefore stable from the very first training step.

\subsection{Feasibility-Guided Exploration}

The $\varepsilon$-greedy policy is guided by a \emph{feasibility mask} to
avoid wasting exploration budget on MODCODs that are clearly out of range
(e.g., 32-APSK~9/10 when SNR = $-1$\,dB guarantees FER = 1):

\begin{equation}
    a_t = \begin{cases}
        \text{Uniform}\bigl(\{i : \text{SNR}_t \geq \theta_i - 2\,\text{dB}\}\bigr)
            & \text{with probability } \varepsilon \\[4pt]
        \arg\max_{a:\,\text{SNR}_t \geq \theta_a - 0.5\,\text{dB}} Q_\theta(\mathbf{s}_t, a)
            & \text{with probability } 1 - \varepsilon
    \end{cases}
\end{equation}

During exploration, the agent samples uniformly from the set of feasible
MODCODs (those within 2\,dB of their threshold). During exploitation, MODCODs
more than 0.5\,dB below their SNR threshold are masked to $-\infty$ before
taking the argmax. This ensures the agent never deliberately selects a MODCOD
it knows will fail.

$\varepsilon$ decays as $\varepsilon_{t+1} = 0.99997\cdot\varepsilon_t$ from
1.0 to a floor of 0.05, reaching the floor after $\approx$100,000 steps
--- equivalent to approximately 50 complete LEO passes at 2,000 steps per pass.
This slow schedule ensures the agent sees a full range of orbital and weather
conditions before committing to an exploitation-only policy.

\subsection{Double DQN: Eliminating Over-Estimation Bias}

Standard Q-learning over-estimates Q-values because $\max_{a'} Q(s', a')$
always picks the highest \emph{estimated} value, which is biased upward.
Double DQN~\cite{vanhasselt2016} decouples action \emph{selection} (online
network $Q_\theta$) from action \emph{evaluation} (target network $Q_{\theta^-}$):
\begin{equation}
    y_t = r_t + \gamma^n \cdot Q_{\theta^-}\!\Bigl(s_{t+n},\;
    \underbrace{\arg\max_{a'} Q_\theta(s_{t+n}, a')}_{\text{policy net selects}}\Bigr)
\end{equation}
where $n = 3$ is the N-step return horizon (Section~\ref{sec:nstep}).
The target network $Q_{\theta^-}$ is updated by \textbf{Polyak (soft) averaging}
after every 200 training steps:
\begin{equation}
    \theta^- \leftarrow \tau\,\theta + (1-\tau)\,\theta^-, \quad \tau = 0.005
\end{equation}
Polyak averaging is smoother than hard copying ($\tau = 1$): the target
network drifts slowly toward the online network, reducing the oscillation
in Bellman targets that causes training instability near threshold boundaries.

\subsection{N-Step Returns}
\label{sec:nstep}

Single-step TD learning ($n = 1$) has high bias: the Bellman target
depends heavily on the estimated value $Q_{\theta^-}(s', a^*)$, which is
inaccurate early in training. Monte-Carlo ($n = \infty$) has high variance:
the return is sensitive to every reward in a long trajectory.

\textbf{N-step returns} with $n = 3$ balance this trade-off. When pushing
a transition to the replay buffer, three consecutive transitions are
accumulated and the compound return is computed:
\begin{equation}
    R_t^{(3)} = r_t + \gamma r_{t+1} + \gamma^2 r_{t+2} +
    \gamma^3 Q_{\theta^-}(s_{t+3},\, a^*_{t+3})
\end{equation}
The stored transition is $(s_t, a_t, R_t^{(3)}, s_{t+3})$.
This gives the agent better credit assignment over a $\sim$300\,ms horizon
(3 steps at 10\,Hz update rate), which covers the full ACM loop latency
(RTT 3.3--13.6\,ms) with margin.

\begin{plainenglish}
With $n=1$, the agent only knows ``I chose MODCOD 14, got reward $r_t$, and the
next state was $s_{t+1}$.''  The future value estimate from $Q_{\theta^-}(s_{t+1})$
is still noisy early in training.  With $n=3$, the agent accumulates three
actual observed rewards before relying on the estimated future value.  This
reduces the bias from a poor value function estimate, at the cost of slightly
higher variance because three random outcomes are compounded.  $n=3$ is a
practical sweet spot for ACM at 10~Hz.
\end{plainenglish}

\begin{conceptbox}{Worked Example: One Complete Training Iteration}
Suppose the buffer has 600 transitions.  The background training thread
draws one minibatch of 64 and performs one gradient step.

\textbf{Step 1 --- Sample from PER buffer.}
The SumTree returns 64 transitions proportional to $|\delta_i|^{0.6}$.
Suppose transition $i$ has:
\[
  s_t = \text{(12.0 dB, pf=0.43, MODCOD 14, BER=}10^{-8}),\quad
  a_t = 14,\quad
  R_t^{(3)} = r_t + 0.95\,r_{t+1} + 0.95^2\,r_{t+2} = 2.71+2.59+2.47 = 7.77
\]

\textbf{Step 2 --- Double DQN Bellman target.}
For next state $s_{t+3}$ (SNR = 13.7\,dB):
\begin{itemize}[nosep,leftmargin=*]
  \item Online network $Q_\theta$ picks best action:
        $a^* = \arg\max_{a'} Q_\theta(s_{t+3},a') = \text{ID~16 (8PSK~8/9)}$
  \item Target network evaluates: $Q_{\theta^-}(s_{t+3}, \text{ID~16}) = 3.22$
  \item Bellman target: $y_i = 7.77 + 0.95^3 \times 3.22 = 7.77 + 2.75 = 10.52$
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Step 3 --- Current Q-value and TD error.}
Online network currently predicts $Q_\theta(s_t, \text{ID~14}) = 9.95$.\\
TD error: $\delta_i = 10.52 - 9.95 = 0.57$.

\textbf{Step 4 --- IS weight.}
PER importance-sampling weight (corrects sampling bias): $w_i = 0.81$.

\textbf{Step 5 --- Huber loss.}
$|\delta_i| = 0.57 < 1$, so $\mathcal{L}_\text{Huber} = \tfrac{1}{2}(0.57)^2 = 0.16$.
Weighted: $w_i \times 0.16 = 0.13$.

\textbf{Step 6 --- Adam gradient step.}
After averaging across all 64 elements, batch loss $\mathcal{L} \approx 0.14$.
The Adam optimiser adjusts all $\sim$270,000 network weights.
The Q-value for ID~14 in state $s_t$ will be slightly larger next forward pass.

\textbf{Step 7 --- Update PER priority.}
New priority: $p_i = (0.57 + 10^{-6})^{0.6} = 0.73$.
Stored back in the SumTree --- this transition will be sampled slightly
more often than before (TD error not yet reduced to zero).

\textbf{Result:} After 2,000 such steps per simulated pass, the Q-function
converges to an accurate estimate of long-term spectral efficiency under
each MODCOD choice.
\end{conceptbox}

\subsection{Prioritized Experience Replay}
\label{sec:per}

Uniform random sampling from the replay buffer treats all transitions equally.
However, transitions near SNR threshold boundaries (where the agent is most
uncertain) are rare and highly informative, while transitions in the middle
of a stable pass are common but uninformative.

\begin{keyinsight}
\textbf{Why uniform replay is wasteful for ACM.}  During a typical 9-minute
LEO pass, the satellite spends most of its time near TCA where the channel is
stable (uninformative transitions) and only a few seconds near the horizon where
MODCOD decisions are critical (informative transitions).  Without PER, the agent
wastes most of its training budget replaying ``easy'' stable-pass transitions.
PER fixes this by replaying hard, uncertain transitions far more often.
\end{keyinsight}

Prioritized Experience Replay (PER,~\cite{schaul2016per}) samples transitions in
proportion to their \emph{TD error}:
\begin{equation}
    P(i) = \frac{p_i^\alpha}{\sum_j p_j^\alpha}, \qquad
    p_i = \bigl|\delta_i\bigr| + \varepsilon_\text{PER},\quad \alpha = 0.6
\end{equation}
where $\delta_i = y_i - Q_\theta(s_i, a_i)$ is the Bellman error for
transition $i$. High-error transitions are replayed more often --- they
represent states where the agent's current Q-function is most wrong.

To correct the bias introduced by non-uniform sampling, importance-sampling
(IS) weights are applied to each loss element:
\begin{equation}
    w_i = \left(\frac{1}{N \cdot P(i)}\right)^\beta,
    \qquad \beta: 0.4 \to 1.0 \text{ (annealed over 20\,000 steps)}
\end{equation}
The weighted loss becomes:
\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{B}|}\sum_{i \in \mathcal{B}}
    w_i \cdot \mathcal{L}_\text{Huber}\!\bigl(y_i,\; Q_\theta(s_i, a_i)\bigr)
\end{equation}
The Huber loss (Smooth-L1) is quadratic for small errors and linear for
large errors, making it robust to outlier transitions with unusually large
TD errors.

The PER data structure is a \textbf{sum-tree}: a binary tree where each leaf
stores the priority of one transition and each internal node stores the sum
of its children. Sampling in $O(\log N)$ time and priority update in
$O(\log N)$ time --- critical for a buffer of 50\,000 transitions.

\subsection{Loss Function and Optimisation}

The complete training step is:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=python, caption={DQN training step: PER sampling, Double DQN target, Huber loss, cosine LR}]
def train_step(self):
    batch, tree_idxs, is_weights = self.replay_buf.sample(64)  # PER sample

    states, actions = ..., ...            # unpack batch
    rewards, next_states, dones = ..., ..., ...

    # Current Q-values for taken actions
    current_q = self.policy_net(states).gather(1, actions).squeeze(1)

    # Double DQN target with N-step gamma
    with torch.no_grad():
        next_actions = self.policy_net(next_states).argmax(1)  # online selects
        next_q = self.target_net(next_states).gather(           # target evaluates
                     1, next_actions.unsqueeze(1)).squeeze(1)
        target_q = rewards + (gamma ** n_step) * next_q * (1 - dones)

    # PER-weighted Huber loss
    td_errors     = (target_q - current_q).detach().cpu().numpy()
    element_loss  = F.smooth_l1_loss(current_q, target_q, reduction='none')
    loss          = (is_weights * element_loss).mean()

    self.optimizer.zero_grad()
    loss.backward()
    torch.nn.utils.clip_grad_norm_(self.policy_net.parameters(), 1.0)
    self.optimizer.step()
    self.scheduler.step()                  # cosine annealing LR update

    self.replay_buf.update_priorities(tree_idxs, td_errors)   # update PER

    # Polyak soft-update target network every 200 steps
    if self.train_steps % 200 == 0:
        for p, tp in zip(self.policy_net.parameters(),
                         self.target_net.parameters()):
            tp.data.copy_(0.005 * p.data + 0.995 * tp.data)
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Cosine Annealing Learning Rate Schedule}

A fixed learning rate of $3\times10^{-4}$ works well early in training but
becomes too large once the policy has converged near a threshold boundary ---
small gradient oscillations cause the MODCOD selection to flicker. Cosine
Annealing with Warm Restarts (Loshchilov \& Hutter, 2017) decays the learning
rate smoothly:
\begin{equation}
    \eta_t = \eta_\text{min} + \frac{1}{2}(\eta_\text{max} - \eta_\text{min})
    \left(1 + \cos\!\left(\frac{\pi\, t_\text{cur}}{T_0}\right)\right)
\end{equation}
with $\eta_\text{max} = 3\times10^{-4}$, $\eta_\text{min} = 3\times10^{-6}$,
$T_0 = 10\,000$ steps, and restart multiplier $T_\text{mult} = 2$.
The LR restarts allow the agent to escape local optima that arise when the
channel distribution shifts (e.g., onset of a rain event mid-pass).

\subsection{Decision Confidence: Q-Gap}

Action confidence is reported as the \textbf{Q-gap} --- the margin between
the best and second-best Q-value:
\begin{equation}
    \text{confidence} = Q_\theta(s, a^*) - \max_{a \neq a^*} Q_\theta(s, a)
\end{equation}
A large Q-gap means the best action is decisively better than the second-best
--- the agent is certain of its choice. A small Q-gap near threshold boundaries
indicates high uncertainty, which the C++ controller can use to apply extra
hysteresis. The confidence value is returned in every ZMQ response.

\section{CNN+LSTM Channel Predictor}

\subsection{The Loop Latency Problem}

Every ACM system faces an inherent time delay between channel quality
measurement and MODCOD taking effect. On a 500\,km LEO link, the round-trip
time ranges from 3.3\,ms at zenith to 13.6\,ms at the horizon. During an
AOS event, the SNR can rise at 1--3\,dB/s; during a rain event onset, it
can fall at 0.5--2\,dB/s. A 13.6\,ms RTT introduces up to $\sim$0.03\,dB
of SNR change per round trip --- small, but compounded across many frames
during fast fades, this leads to systematic MODCOD overshoot.

\subsection{CNN+LSTM Architecture}

The predictor uses a \textbf{1-D CNN} followed by an \textbf{LSTM}~\cite{zhang2022,mdpi2024snr}.
The CNN acts as a learned filter that extracts short-term patterns --- rate of
change, scintillation oscillations, monotonic fading --- from the raw SNR
sequence. The LSTM then models long-range dependencies across the pass
trajectory~\cite{wang2019wcnc}. This two-stage design separates local feature
extraction from temporal sequence modelling:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=python, caption={CNN+LSTM SNR predictor (PyTorch)}]
class CNNLSTMPredictor(nn.Module):
    """
    CNN extracts local patterns; LSTM models pass-level trajectory.
    Input:  SNR sequence [batch, seq_len=32, 1]
    Output: predicted SNR at t+1..t+10 [batch, pred_steps=10]
    """
    def __init__(self, hidden_dim=64, cnn_channels=32, cnn_kernel=5):
        super().__init__()
        # Two CNN layers: extract rate-of-change and oscillation patterns
        self.cnn = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Conv1d(1, cnn_channels, kernel_size=cnn_kernel,
                      padding=cnn_kernel//2),
            nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Conv1d(cnn_channels, cnn_channels, kernel_size=3, padding=1),
            nn.ReLU(),
        )
        # 2-layer LSTM: models long-range pass trajectory
        self.lstm = nn.LSTM(cnn_channels, hidden_dim, num_layers=2,
                            batch_first=True, dropout=0.1)
        # Output: predict SNR at t+1 ... t+10
        self.fc = nn.Sequential(
            nn.Linear(hidden_dim, 32), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(32, 10)   # 10 steps ahead
        )

    def forward(self, x):
        # x: [B, seq=32, 1] -> CNN expects [B, 1, seq]
        cnn_out  = self.cnn(x.permute(0, 2, 1))       # [B, 32, seq]
        lstm_out, _ = self.lstm(cnn_out.permute(0, 2, 1))  # [B, seq, 64]
        return self.fc(lstm_out[:, -1, :])             # [B, 10]
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Online Training on Live Passes}

The predictor trains continuously on the live SNR time series during each
pass. Every 10 new SNR measurements, a batch of sliding-window supervised
pairs is constructed from the accumulated history and 4 gradient steps are
taken:

\begin{equation}
    \mathcal{L}_\text{pred} = \frac{1}{B} \sum_{i=1}^{B}
    \bigl\|\hat{\mathbf{y}}_i - \mathbf{y}_i\bigr\|^2,
    \quad
    \mathbf{y}_i = [\text{SNR}_{t_i+1},\ldots,\text{SNR}_{t_i+10}]
\end{equation}

where the 10-step prediction target covers the full RTT range at all
elevations. At 10\,Hz update rate, 10 steps = 1\,second of look-ahead,
which exceeds the maximum RTT of 13.6\,ms by a factor of 70 --- providing
ample margin.

The predictor adapts to the specific orbital geometry of each pass (overhead
vs low-elevation), each rain environment, and each hardware configuration.
It learns the current pass characteristics in the first 30--40\,s after AOS.

The predictor saves its weights on shutdown and reloads them at startup,
so accumulated knowledge from previous passes is retained across sessions.

\section{Reward Function Design}
\label{sec:reward}

\subsection{Design Principles}

The reward must encode three competing objectives in a single scalar:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Maximise throughput:} Use the highest possible MODCOD.
    \item \textbf{Maintain QoS:} BER must stay below $10^{-7}$ (the
          DVB-S2 quasi-error-free operating point).
    \item \textbf{Maintain stability:} Avoid excessive MODCOD switching.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Reward Function}
\label{sec:reward_formula}

The reward combines a throughput term, a link margin score, a hard BER QoS
gate, an exponential FER penalty, and a switching cost:
\begin{equation}
    r_t =
    \underbrace{\eta(m_t)}_{\text{(A) throughput}}
    \cdot\;
    \underbrace{\sigma\!\bigl(2(\text{SNR}_t - \theta_{m_t})\bigr)}_{\text{(B) link margin}}
    \cdot\;
    \underbrace{\mathbf{1}[\text{BER}_t < 10^{-7}]}_{\text{(C) QoS gate}}
    \;-\;
    \underbrace{\bigl(e^{10\cdot\text{FER}_t} - 1\bigr)}_{\text{(D) FER penalty}}
    \;-\;
    \underbrace{\lambda\,\mathbf{1}[m_t \neq m_{t-1}]}_{\text{(E) switch}}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma(x) = 1/(1+e^{-x})$ is the sigmoid function and $\lambda = 0.05$.

\begin{plainenglish}
The reward is designed to balance five competing objectives in a single number:

\textbf{(A) Throughput:} Choosing a higher MODCOD (more bits/symbol) directly
increases the reward.  The agent is always incentivised to use the fastest
MODCOD the channel can support.

\textbf{(B) Link margin:} Multiplied by a smooth ``confidence'' score: 1.0 when
the SNR is comfortably above the MODCOD threshold, and approaching 0 when SNR is
right at the edge.  This prevents the agent from greedily choosing a MODCOD whose
threshold it can barely meet.

\textbf{(C) QoS gate:} If the bit error rate is too high ($> 10^{-7}$, the
DVB-S2 ``quasi-error-free'' limit), the entire throughput--margin product drops to
zero.  No credit at all for a MODCOD that is violating the quality target.

\textbf{(D) FER penalty:} An exponential penalty for frame errors that grows
catastrophically ($\gg 100$) if many frames are failing.  This makes link failure
extremely unattractive, strongly pushing the agent to downshift \emph{before}
errors accumulate.

\textbf{(E) Switch cost:} A small penalty ($\lambda = 0.05$) for every MODCOD
change, preventing unnecessary ``ping-pong'' switching near SNR threshold boundaries.
\end{plainenglish}

\textbf{Term (A): Spectral efficiency $\eta(m_t)$}\\
$\eta(m)$ is the information bits per symbol for MODCOD $m$, ranging from
0.49\,(QPSK~1/4) to 5.85\,(32APSK~9/10). This is the base incentive to
select the highest-throughput MODCOD.

\textbf{Term (B): Sigmoid link margin $\sigma(2(\text{SNR} - \theta))$}\\
The sigmoid provides a smooth, differentiable quality score: 1.0 when SNR
is well above the MODCOD threshold, 0.5 exactly at the threshold, and
approaching 0 when well below.

\textbf{Term (C): Hard BER QoS gate $\mathbf{1}[\text{BER} < 10^{-7}]$}\\
If BER exceeds the DVB-S2 quasi-error-free target of $10^{-7}$, the entire
throughput-margin product is set to zero. The agent receives \emph{no credit}
for spectral efficiency when the link violates QoS. FER and BER can diverge:
short bursts of errors may yield acceptable FER but unacceptable BER, so
both metrics are necessary.

\textbf{Term (D): Exponential FER penalty $e^{10\cdot\text{FER}} - 1$}\\
The exponential penalty is negligible at FER $< 0.01$, equals 1.72 at
FER = 0.1, and equals 147 at FER = 0.3, making severe link degradation
\emph{catastrophically} unrewarding and strongly incentivising a pre-emptive
downshift before FER rises.

\textbf{Term (E): Switching penalty $\lambda = 0.05$}\\
Each MODCOD switch costs 0.05\,bits/sym ($\sim$1\,\% of median efficiency),
preventing ping-pong oscillation near SNR thresholds without over-penalising
necessary switches during fast fades.

\section{Offline Pre-Training and Deployment Workflow}
\label{sec:pretrain}

\subsection{Offline Pre-Training}

Before deployment, the DQN is pre-trained offline using the pure-Python
simulation to produce a model checkpoint that already understands the full
DVB-S2 MODCOD ladder. The training scenario uses an SNR \emph{sweep} that
steps through the range $-3$\,dB to 16\,dB --- the actual SNR range measured
at the GNU Radio SNR Estimator during live GRC operation:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=bash, caption={Offline DQN pre-training command}]
# Delete any old checkpoint to start fresh
rm -f dqn_acm_model.pt snr_predictor.pt

python3 examples/acm_loopback_sim.py \
    --scenario sweep --snr-start -3 --snr-end 16 \
    --use-ai --passes 50
\end{lstlisting}

Each pass comprises approximately 2,000 simulation steps at 1\,Hz, giving
100,000 total training steps. $\varepsilon$ decays from 1.0 to 0.05 over
this full budget, ensuring the agent explores the entire SNR space before
committing to exploitation. Training completes in approximately 15--20 minutes
on CPU. The resulting checkpoint (\texttt{dqn\_acm\_model.pt}) is saved to
the project root directory.

\subsection{What Happens When the Predictor Is Wrong?}

If the CNN+LSTM predictor over-estimates future SNR (e.g., an unexpected rain
cell appears), the DQN may select a MODCOD that fails.  Two safety mechanisms
prevent this from becoming catastrophic:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Feasibility masking:} Only MODCODs within 2\,dB of the
        \emph{current measured} SNR (not the predicted value) are eligible
        for exploration.  Even if the predictor is optimistic, the agent
        cannot select a wildly infeasible MODCOD.
  \item \textbf{Exponential FER penalty:} If frames fail due to an
        over-optimistic selection, the reward drops to $-147$ or worse.
        This is so severe that the DQN quickly learns to add a safety margin
        when the SNR trend is non-monotonic or the predictor confidence is
        low.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{importantnote}
The DQN is not perfect during the first 10--20 live passes while
$\varepsilon > 0.3$.  The offline pre-training gives it a reasonable
starting policy, but random exploration still causes occasional frame
failures.  The exponential FER penalty ensures these errors are punished
strongly enough that the agent learns within a few passes not to repeat
them.  By pass 50 ($\varepsilon \approx 0.07$), performance is stable
and consistently outperforms the rule-based baseline on all tested scenarios.
\end{importantnote}

\chapter{LEO Satellite Channel Model}
\label{chap:channel}
% =============================================================================

\begin{keyinsight}
\textbf{What this chapter covers.}
The physics-based LEO channel simulation (\texttt{leo\_channel\_model.py}).
This model is used both by the GNU Radio flowgraph (as the \texttt{leo\_channel}
block) and by the DQN pre-training procedure.  It covers orbital mechanics, free-space
path loss, Doppler shift, atmospheric rain attenuation (ITU-R P.618-13), and
Rician multipath fading --- the five phenomena that make a LEO X-Band link
fundamentally different from a static GEO link.
\end{keyinsight}

\section{Overview and Importance}

A realistic channel model is essential for any meaningful simulation. The LEO
channel model (\texttt{leo\_channel\_model.py}) simulates the complete physics
of a 500~km altitude satellite pass, following ITU-R Recommendations~P.618-13,
P.838-3, P.839-4, P.676-12, P.840-8, and P.681-11~\cite{itu_p618,itu_p838,
itu_p839,itu_p676,itu_p840,itu_p681}, with time-correlated fading based on
the Lutz two-state model~\cite{lutz1991} and 3GPP TR~38.811 NTN parameters~\cite{3gpp_tr38811}.
The scenario matches the X-Band LEO link studied in~\cite{magro2025,altunc2015,wang2022sensors}.

\section{Orbital Mechanics and Pass Geometry}

For a circular orbit at altitude $h = 500$~km, the orbital velocity and period
are determined by Newtonian gravity:
\begin{align}
    r_\text{orbit} &= R_E + h = 6378 + 500 = 6878~\text{km} \\
    v_\text{orbit} &= \sqrt{\frac{GM_E}{r_\text{orbit}}} \approx 7.62~\text{km/s}
\end{align}
The elevation angle $\theta(t)$ over the station changes as the satellite moves
in its orbit. A pass is active when $\theta > 5^\circ$ (horizon mask). The
slant range $d(\theta)$ is:
\begin{equation}
    d(\theta) = \sqrt{(R_E + h)^2 - R_E^2\cos^2\theta} - R_E\sin\theta
\end{equation}

\section{Free-Space Path Loss}

The free-space path loss (FSPL) varies with slant range and frequency:
\begin{equation}
    \text{FSPL}(d) = 20\log_{10}(d) + 20\log_{10}(f) + 20\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{4\pi}{c}\right)
\end{equation}
At X-Band ($f = 8.025$~GHz), FSPL ranges from 164.5~dB at zenith (d = 500 km)
to 176.7~dB at the horizon (d $\approx$ 2500 km) --- a swing of 12.2~dB per
pass. This swing directly translates to $\sim$12~dB SNR variation, requiring
the ACM system to traverse the full MODCOD range from QPSK~1/4 to 32APSK~9/10
within a single 9-minute pass.

\begin{plainenglish}
FSPL is fundamentally about geometry: a radio signal spreads out in all directions
as a sphere, so its energy density falls as $1/d^2$ (i.e.\ $20\log_{10}(d)$ in
dB).  When the satellite is directly overhead, the distance is 500~km.  At
$5^\circ$ elevation (near the horizon), it is roughly 2500~km --- five times
farther.  $20\log_{10}(5) \approx 14$~dB is therefore the approximate path-loss
swing, which is precisely the dominant source of the 12.2~dB SNR variation
observed during a pass.
\end{plainenglish}

\section{Doppler Shift}

The radial velocity component between spacecraft and ground station determines
the Doppler shift:
\begin{equation}
    f_d(t) = \frac{v_\text{radial}(t)}{c} \cdot f_c
\end{equation}
At X-Band, the maximum Doppler is:
\begin{equation}
    f_{d,\max} = \frac{7620}{3\times10^8} \times 8.025\times10^9 \approx 188~\text{kHz}
\end{equation}
The Doppler rate (frequency chirp) peaks near the horizon and zero-crosses at
TCA, reaching approximately 350~Hz/s near the horizon. The PL Sync block must
track this continuous frequency change (see Section~\ref{sec:pl_sync}).

\section{Atmospheric Attenuation}
\label{sec:atm_atten}

The channel model implements four atmospheric impairment layers, each following
the relevant ITU-R Recommendation.

\subsection{Rain Attenuation (ITU-R P.618-13 + P.838-3 + P.839-4)}

Rain attenuation is computed per the six-step procedure of
ITU-R P.618-13~\cite{itu_p618} Annex~1 Section~2.2:

\textbf{Step 1 — Specific attenuation} (ITU-R P.838-3~\cite{itu_p838}):
\begin{equation}
    \gamma_R = k \cdot R^\alpha \quad \text{(dB/km)}
\end{equation}
For X-Band (8.025~GHz) circular polarisation, the P.838-3 coefficients are
$k = 0.0038$, $\alpha = 1.240$ (averaged from tabulated $k_H = 0.00395$,
$\alpha_H = 1.228$, $k_V = 0.00365$, $\alpha_V = 1.254$ at 8~GHz).

\textbf{Step 2 — Rain height} (ITU-R P.839-4~\cite{itu_p839}):
\begin{equation}
    h_R = h_0 + 0.36~\text{km}
\end{equation}
where $h_0$ is the 0°C isotherm height derived from the P.839-4 global table.
For a mid-latitude station ($\phi = 51.5°$N), $h_0 \approx 2.68$~km, giving
$h_R \approx 3.04$~km.

\textbf{Steps 3--5 — Slant path and effective length}:
\begin{align}
    L_S &= (h_R - h_S)\,/\,\sin\theta       && \text{slant path (km)} \\
    d_0 &= L_S \cos\theta                    && \text{horizontal projection} \\
    r_{0.01} &= \left(1 + 0.78\sqrt{d_0/\gamma_R}
               - 0.38(1 - e^{-2d_0})\right)^{-1} && \text{reduction factor}
\end{align}
The effective path $L_E$ is then computed from the vertical adjustment angle
$\zeta = \arctan((h_R - h_S)/(d_0 r_{0.01}))$ relative to the elevation $\theta$.

\textbf{Step 6:} $A_\text{rain} = \gamma_R \cdot L_E$ (dB).

\subsection{Atmospheric Gas Absorption (ITU-R P.676-12)}

Oxygen and water-vapour absorption~\cite{itu_p676} is computed from the zenith
opacity and scaled by the slant path:
\begin{equation}
    A_\text{gas}(\theta) = \frac{A_\text{gas,zenith}}{\sin\theta}
\end{equation}
At 8.025~GHz with standard atmosphere ($\rho_w = 7.5$~g/m$^3$):
$A_\text{gas,zenith} \approx 0.04~\text{(O}_2) + 0.16~\text{(H}_2\text{O)}
= 0.20$~dB.

\subsection{Cloud and Fog Attenuation (ITU-R P.840-8)}

For non-zero cloud liquid water path~\cite{itu_p840}:
\begin{equation}
    A_\text{cloud}(\theta) = \frac{K_L \cdot L_{\rm cloud}}{\sin\theta}
\end{equation}
where $K_L \approx 0.28$~dB\,m$^2$/kg at 8~GHz and $L_\text{cloud}$ is the
integrated cloud liquid water path (kg/m$^2$).

\subsection{Tropospheric Scintillation (ITU-R P.618-13 §2.4)}

The standard deviation of scintillation amplitude is computed per P.618-13
Section~2.4~\cite{itu_p618}:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_x(f, \theta) = \sigma_\text{ref} \cdot g(f)^{11/12}
                          \cdot \left(\sin\theta\right)^{-11/12}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_\text{ref} = 0.5187 \cdot N_\text{wet}^{0.4588}$ (\% power)
depends on the wet refractivity $N_\text{wet}$, and $g(f) \approx (f/10)^{7/12}$
is a frequency scaling factor. For $N_\text{wet} = 42.5$~N-units at 8.025~GHz ($g(f) = (8.025/10)^{7/12} \approx 0.880$):
$\sigma_x \approx 0.11$~dB at zenith, rising to $\approx 1.04$~dB at 5°
elevation (the $(\sin\theta)^{-11/12}$ scaling causes a factor of $\approx 9.4$
amplification between zenith and 5° elevation). Scintillation is generated as a time-correlated process using an
AR(1) model with correlation time $\tau_c = 2$~s, consistent with the typical
X-band scintillation bandwidth of 0.1--0.5~Hz.

\section{Rician Fading}
\label{sec:rician}

The small-scale fading component follows a Rician distribution.  The K-factor
(ratio of LOS power to scattered power) is modelled as a function of elevation
angle per ITU-R P.681-11~\cite{itu_p681} and the two-state model of
Lutz et al.~\cite{lutz1991}:
\begin{equation}
    K_\text{dB}(\theta) = 10 + 10\left(\frac{\theta}{90^\circ}\right)~\text{dB}
\end{equation}
This gives $K \approx 10.6$~dB at $\theta = 5^\circ$ (near horizon),
$K \approx 15$~dB at $\theta = 45^\circ$ (mid-pass), and $K \approx 20$~dB
at zenith (dominant LOS). The range 10--20~dB is consistent with P.681-11
Table~1 for open/suburban X-band fixed ground stations, where the high
carrier frequency and fixed dish antenna maintain a strong LOS component
even near the horizon. The fading is generated
with an AR(1) time-correlated process (correlation time $\tau_c = 2$~s,
consistent with 3GPP TR~38.811~\cite{3gpp_tr38811} NTN fading parameters).

\section{Overview: What the Flowgraph Does}

The GRC flowgraph (\texttt{examples/acm\_loopback.grc}) implements the
correct DVB-S2 ACM architecture per ETSI EN~302~307-1 entirely in software.
The satellite transmitter generates random TS data, encodes and modulates it
to DVB-S2 PL frames, and passes them through the physics-based LEO channel
model.  The ground station receiver demodulates, decodes, and---critically---
makes the MODCOD decision at the \emph{receiver} side.  The selected MODCOD
is returned to the satellite via a simulated uplink return channel (modelled
as a message port connection with RTT/2 propagation delay applied inside the
ACM Controller block).  This is the architecturally correct feedback loop for
DVB-S2 ACM: the terminal (ground station) measures channel quality and
instructs the transmitter (satellite) which MODCOD to use for the next
transmission window.

Running the flowgraph allows you to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Observe real-time MODCOD switching as the channel SNR changes
    \item See the constellation evolve (QPSK $\to$ 8PSK $\to$ 16APSK $\to$ 32APSK)
          as SNR improves
    \item Compare the TX and RX spectra to see the effect of Doppler and noise
    \item Watch the carrier recovery work on the constellation plot (locked vs
          unlocked)
    \item Verify the ACM feedback loop latency and stability
    \item Switch the ACM policy between rule-based and DQN live
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Step 1: Data Generation (Random Source)}

\textbf{Block}: \texttt{analog\_random\_source\_x} \\
\textbf{Output}: \texttt{uint8} stream at $\sim$1~Mbyte/s

The random source produces a continuous stream of uniformly distributed bytes
(0--255). Each byte represents one bit (only the LSB is used). This simulates
user data. In a real deployment, this source would be replaced by an MPEG-TS
stream from a modulator or a data interface.

The source runs without a rate limit --- it produces data as fast as the
downstream blocks can consume it. The throttle block (Step 6) is the actual
rate controller.

\subsection{Step 2: Baseband Framing (BB Framer)}

\textbf{Block}: \texttt{dvbs2acm\_bb\_framer\_acm} \\
\textbf{Input}: raw \texttt{uint8} bits \\
\textbf{Output}: \texttt{uint8} BBFRAME (header + payload)

This block is the entry point of the DVB-S2 protocol stack. It performs three
functions in sequence:

\textbf{(a) Check for new MODCOD:} The block has a message input port
(\texttt{modcod\_in}) connected to the ACM Controller output. Before processing
each frame, it checks whether a new MODCOD has been selected. If so, it updates
its internal MODCOD state, which determines how many data bits to include in the
next frame ($k_\text{BCH}$ depends on the MODCOD).

\textbf{(b) Construct the BBHEADER:} The 10-byte header is built according to
ETSI EN~302~307-1 Annex C. The MATYPE-2 byte encodes the MODCOD ID, making the
frame self-describing. The DFL (Data Field Length) field tells the FEC encoder
and ultimately the receiver how many of the following bits are user data vs
padding.

\textbf{(c) Attach stream tags:} The block calls \texttt{add\_item\_tag()} to
attach \texttt{"modcod"}, \texttt{"frame\_start"}, and \texttt{"pilots"} tags
at the first sample of the output frame. These tags propagate automatically
through GNU Radio's scheduler to all downstream blocks.

\textbf{What you see in operation:} Each time the ACM Controller sends a new
MODCOD, the BB Framer immediately switches. The next output frame uses the new
MODCOD, with the updated BBHEADER and the new stream tag value.

\subsection{Step 3: FEC Encoding (FEC Encoder)}

\textbf{Block}: \texttt{dvbs2acm\_fec\_encoder\_acm} \\
\textbf{Input}: \texttt{uint8} BBFRAME ($k_\text{BCH}$ bits) \\
\textbf{Output}: \texttt{uint8} LDPC codeword (always 64,800 bits)

The FEC encoder applies the two-stage DVB-S2 error correction to the payload:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item The BBFRAME's $k_\text{BCH}$ bits are passed through the BCH encoder,
          which appends $n_\text{BCH} - k_\text{BCH}$ parity bits (the BCH
          ``check'' bits). These parity bits allow the receiver to detect and
          correct up to $t = 8$ or 12 bit errors after LDPC decoding.
    \item The BCH codeword ($n_\text{BCH}$ bits) is then passed through the
          LDPC encoder, which appends $64800 - n_\text{BCH}$ LDPC parity bits.
          The LDPC code is the primary error correction mechanism, operating
          very close to the Shannon limit.
\end{enumerate}

The output is always exactly 64,800 bits regardless of MODCOD. The MODCOD only
determines how many of those bits are information ($k_\text{BCH}$) vs redundancy.

\textbf{Internally:} The block caches 28 gr-dtv sub-flowgraphs (one per MODCOD).
When a \texttt{"modcod"} stream tag arrives on the input, the block selects the
corresponding sub-flowgraph and uses its encoder. This is the mechanism that
implements ACM at the FEC level.

\subsection{Step 4: Constellation Mapping (Modulator)}

\textbf{Block}: \texttt{dvbs2acm\_modulator\_acm} \\
\textbf{Input}: \texttt{uint8} (64,800 bits per frame) \\
\textbf{Output}: \texttt{complex64} IQ symbols

The modulator groups the 64,800 bits into groups of $B$ bits (where $B = 2$
for QPSK, 3 for 8PSK, 4 for 16APSK, 5 for 32APSK) and maps each group to a
complex IQ coordinate on the ETSI-specified constellation.

A copy of the IQ output is sent to the \textbf{TX Constellation Sink} (GUI),
showing the clean, noiseless constellation at this point. This is a useful
debugging reference: if the RX constellation (after the channel and PL Sync)
matches this plot, carrier recovery is working correctly.

\textbf{What you can observe:} When the ACM Controller increases the MODCOD
from QPSK (ID~4) to 8PSK (ID~13), the constellation display immediately changes
from 4 points to 8 points. At 32APSK (IDs 24--28), three concentric rings of
4, 12, and 16 points are visible.

\subsection{Step 5: Physical Layer Framing (PL Framer)}

\textbf{Block}: \texttt{dvbs2acm\_pl\_framer\_acm} \\
\textbf{Input}: \texttt{complex64} IQ symbols \\
\textbf{Output}: \texttt{complex64} complete PL frame

The PL framer adds the DVB-S2 Physical Layer wrapper around the data symbols:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Prepend the PLHEADER (90 symbols):} The 26-symbol SOF
          followed by the 64-symbol PLSCODE. These are always BPSK (binary
          phase shift keying), regardless of the data modulation order.
    \item \textbf{Insert pilot blocks:} Every 16 data slots (1440 symbols),
          a 36-symbol pilot block of all-ones BPSK is inserted. For a QPSK~1/2
          frame, this means approximately 22 pilot blocks per frame.
    \item \textbf{Apply Gold scrambling:} Every symbol (including header and
          pilots) is multiplied by the complex Gold code sequence $c_n$.
          This randomises the spectrum and prevents spectral lines.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Effect on frame length:} Without pilots, a QPSK frame is
$90 + 32400 = 32490$ symbols. With pilots (22 blocks of 36), it grows to
$32490 + 22 \times 36 = 33282$ symbols. The frame length varies with MODCOD
because higher-order constellations carry more bits/symbol and thus fewer
symbols.

\subsection{Step 6: Rate Limiting (Throttle)}

\textbf{Block}: \texttt{blocks\_throttle2} \\
\textbf{Sample rate}: 1,000,000 samples/second (1~Msps)

In a real DVB-S2 system, the sample rate is determined by the hardware DAC/ADC.
In software loopback, without a throttle, GNU Radio would run as fast as the
CPU allows --- potentially thousands of times faster than real time, which
would cause buffer overflows and 100\% CPU usage.

The throttle block measures wall-clock time and limits the output rate to match
the configured sample rate. At 1~Msps, one complete QPSK~1/2 PL frame
(33,282 symbols) takes approximately 33.3~ms to transmit --- giving the ACM
feedback loop about 33 opportunities per second to update the MODCOD.

\subsection{Step 7: Channel Model}

\textbf{Block 1}: \texttt{dvbs2acm\_leo\_channel} \\
\textbf{Block 2}: \texttt{channels\_channel\_model}

The two channel blocks in series model the complete satellite link:

\textbf{LEO Channel Model:} Applies time-varying complex gain representing the
complete satellite pass physics:
\begin{equation}
    y[n] = G(t_n) \cdot e^{j2\pi f_d(t_n) \cdot n / f_s} \cdot x[n]
\end{equation}
where $G(t_n)$ is the combined path loss + rain + fading amplitude at time
$t_n$ (updated every output frame), and $f_d(t_n)$ is the current Doppler
frequency offset (up to $\pm$188~kHz at X-Band). The block also publishes a
\texttt{"channel\_state"} message with elevation angle, current SNR, and
Doppler value --- visible in the Message Debug block and consumed by the
Orbit Visualizer block.

Key GRC parameters: \texttt{altitude\_km=500}, \texttt{freq\_hz=8.025e9},
\texttt{time\_acceleration=10.0} (simulated pass runs 10$\times$ faster than
real time), \texttt{fading\_coherence\_s=30.0} (AR(1) Rician K-factor
correlation time), \texttt{rain\_rate\_mm\_hr} (driven by the Rain Rate
slider), \texttt{noise\_temp\_k=150}, \texttt{rx\_gain\_dbi=37},
\texttt{tx\_power\_dbw=0}, \texttt{min\_elevation\_deg=5}.

\textbf{AWGN Channel:} The standard GNU Radio \texttt{channels\_channel\_model}
block adds $\mathcal{CN}(0, \sigma^2)$ complex Gaussian noise:
\begin{equation}
    r[n] = y[n] + w[n], \qquad w[n] \sim \mathcal{CN}(0, \sigma^2)
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Physics-based ACM mode (default).}
In the current flowgraph the \texttt{noise\_voltage} parameter is set to
\texttt{'0'}, so the AWGN block passes the signal through without adding noise
($\sigma = 0$).  The link SNR is supplied entirely by the LEO Channel Model,
which computes a physics-based SNR from orbital geometry, free-space path loss,
and ITU-R atmospheric impairments and delivers it to the ACM Controller via the
\texttt{channel\_state} message port.  The IQ-path SNR Estimator block is
therefore not the primary SNR source for ACM decisions in this mode.

\paragraph{Manual AWGN mode (for hardware testing).}
If \texttt{noise\_voltage} is set to $\sigma = 10^{-\text{snr\_db}/20}$ the
slider injects calibrated AWGN and the SNR Estimator measures it from the IQ
signal.  Because the DVB-S2 signal chain (BB Framer $\to$ FEC $\to$ Modulator
$\to$ PL Framer) produces IQ symbols with an RMS amplitude of approximately
0.148 (signal power $\approx 0.022$, i.e.\ $-16.6$\,dB relative to unit
power), the SNR measured at the Estimator is:
\begin{equation}
    \text{SNR}_\text{meas} \approx \text{snr\_db} - 16.6\;\text{dB}
    \quad (\text{at TCA, LEO channel amplitude} = 1.0)
    \label{eq:snr_offset}
\end{equation}
The LEO channel adds an additional $-12.2$\,dB path-loss variation from TCA
to the horizon, so with \texttt{snr\_db}\,=\,33 the measured SNR spans
$\approx$\,4.2\,dB at AOS/LOS ($5^\circ$ elevation) to $\approx$\,16.4\,dB
at TCA --- covering QPSK~1/4 through 16APSK~8/9 across one pass.
The AWGN block can also introduce a static frequency offset to exercise the
PL Sync carrier recovery loop.

\textbf{Fan-out from AWGN output:} The noisy received signal is distributed to
five destinations simultaneously (GNU Radio handles this via internal buffer
copies):
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item PL Sync (signal processing)
    \item SNR Estimator (signal processing)
    \item Qt Frequency Sink, port 0 (pre-sync spectrum display)
    \item Qt Time Sink (amplitude envelope display)
    \item Qt Waterfall Sink (time-frequency display)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Step 8: Physical Layer Synchronisation (PL Sync)}

\textbf{Block}: \texttt{dvbs2acm\_pl\_sync\_acm} \\
\textbf{Input}: noisy \texttt{complex64} IQ \\
\textbf{Output}: descrambled, carrier-corrected \texttt{complex64} IQ

This is the most critical block in the receiver. It transforms a noisy,
frequency-offset, unsynchronised IQ stream into a clean, frame-aligned,
carrier-corrected symbol stream. The block operates in two phases:

\textbf{Acquisition phase} (runs until locked):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Applies current frequency correction estimate (zero initially)
    \item Accumulates incoming samples in a search buffer
    \item Every 512 samples, runs the SOF sliding correlator
    \item When the correlation peak exceeds the threshold: saves the SOF phase,
          marks the frame boundary, transitions to \emph{locked} phase
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Locked phase} (normal operation):
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Apply per-sample correction: $r'[k] = r[k] \cdot e^{-j(\hat{\phi}+\hat{f}\cdot k)}$
    \item Re-correlate with SOF at the expected position $\to$ update
          $\hat{f}$ (frequency) and $\hat{\phi}$ (phase)
    \item Apply Gold descrambling to full frame
    \item Find pilot blocks and apply pilot PLL corrections
    \item Decode PLSCODE $\to$ extract MODCOD ID
    \item Attach \texttt{"modcod"} and \texttt{"frame\_start"} stream tags
    \item Run M\&M TED on data symbols $\to$ compute timing offset metric
    \item Publish \texttt{"frame\_info"} message (MODCOD, freq estimate, timing)
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{What you observe on the RX Constellation Sink:} Before lock, the
constellation is a spinning ring or ellipse (uncorrected Doppler). During
acquisition (0.1--2 seconds), the ring stops spinning and the four QPSK points
begin to emerge. After full lock, the constellation shows clean, well-separated
decision regions matching the TX constellation display.

The PL Sync output is distributed to:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Demodulator (continue signal processing)
    \item Qt Constellation Sink --- RX (visual lock confirmation)
    \item Qt Frequency Sink, port 1 (post-sync spectrum for comparison with pre-sync)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Step 9: SNR Estimation (SNR Estimator)}

\textbf{Block}: \texttt{dvbs2acm\_snr\_estimator} \\
\textbf{Input}: received IQ (from AWGN, before PL Sync) \\
\textbf{Output}: \texttt{"snr\_out"} message port

The SNR estimator receives the \emph{raw} noisy channel output (not the
PL Sync output) so it can measure the channel quality independently of the
carrier recovery quality. This is important: if the PL Sync is not yet locked,
the SNR can still be measured blindly using the M2M4 algorithm.

The block processes incoming IQ in batches of approximately 1000 samples
(selectable), applies both estimation algorithms, fuses them through the
Kalman filter, and publishes an SNR measurement message every $\sim$8 frames.

\textbf{Message format} (PMT dictionary):
\begin{lstlisting}[language=python, caption={SNR Estimator output message format}]
{
    "snr_db":      12.4,   # Kalman-filtered SNR estimate
    "snr_pilot":   12.8,   # Pilot-MMSE estimate (if pilots present)
    "snr_m2m4":    12.1,   # Blind M2M4 estimate
    "frame_count": 847,    # Frames processed since start
}
\end{lstlisting}

\subsection{Step 10: Soft-Decision Demodulation (Demodulator)}

\textbf{Block}: \texttt{dvbs2acm\_demodulator\_acm} \\
\textbf{Input}: descrambled \texttt{complex64} IQ (from PL Sync) \\
\textbf{Output}: \texttt{float32} LLR stream (64,800 values per frame)

The demodulator reads the \texttt{"modcod"} stream tag from the PL Sync output
to determine which constellation and code rate are in use. It then:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Removes the pilot symbols from the stream (they are not data symbols
          and must not be demodulated as data)
    \item Computes the max-log LLR for each bit position of each remaining
          symbol using the received IQ sample $y$, the ETSI constellation $\mathcal{C}$,
          and the noise variance $\hat{\sigma}^2$ from the SNR estimator
    \item Reverses the DVB-S2 bit interleaving (the standard interleaves bits
          across symbols for burst error protection; the deinterleaver restores
          natural bit order)
    \item Outputs 64,800 LLR values for the FEC decoder
\end{enumerate}

A positive LLR means the receiver is confident the bit is 0; a negative LLR
means the bit is likely 1. The \emph{magnitude} of the LLR encodes confidence:
large $|L|$ means high confidence, $L \approx 0$ means the channel obliterated
this bit.

\subsection{Step 11: FEC Decoding (FEC Decoder)}

\textbf{Block}: \texttt{dvbs2acm\_fec\_decoder\_acm} \\
\textbf{Input}: \texttt{float32} LLRs (64,800 per frame) \\
\textbf{Output}: \texttt{uint8} decoded information bits ($k_\text{BCH}$ per frame)

The FEC decoder receives the 64,800 LLRs and attempts to recover the
$k_\text{BCH}$ original information bits using the two-stage process described
in Section~\ref{sec:fec_decoder}. For each frame, it publishes a
\texttt{"ber\_out"} message with performance metrics.

\textbf{Consuming the output:} In the loopback flowgraph, the decoded bits go
to a \texttt{null\_sink} (since the random source bits are not archived for
BER comparison). In a production system, this output would be routed to an
MPEG-TS demultiplexer or a data interface.

\subsection{Step 12: ACM Feedback and Control Loop}

This is where the system closes the ACM loop. Two distinct message paths run
in parallel:

\textbf{Path A --- Signal-plane tags:} The \texttt{"modcod"} stream tag travels
from the BB Framer through FEC Encoder $\to$ Modulator $\to$ PL Framer $\to$
Channel $\to$ PL Sync $\to$ Demodulator $\to$ FEC Decoder. Every block along
this path reads the tag to know which MODCOD is in use for the current frame.
This path is \emph{in-band} and has exactly zero latency relative to the data.

\textbf{Path B --- Control-plane messages:} This is the ACM feedback loop:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{SNR Estimator} publishes SNR measurements on \texttt{snr\_out}
          every $\sim$8 frames ($\sim$260~ms).
    \item \textbf{FEC Decoder} publishes BER/FER on \texttt{ber\_out} every frame.
    \item \textbf{ACM Feedback} (\texttt{dvbs2acm\_acm\_feedback}) receives both
          message streams. It rate-limits to 10~Hz, computes the SNR trend
          $\Delta\text{SNR}$, and publishes a consolidated report on
          \texttt{feedback\_out}.
    \item \textbf{ACM Controller} (\texttt{dvbs2acm\_acm\_controller}) receives
          the report and selects the next MODCOD:
          \begin{itemize}[nosep]
              \item \emph{Rule-based mode}: Threshold lookup with hysteresis.
              \item \emph{AI mode}: ZMQ call to the DQN agent.
          \end{itemize}
          If the selected MODCOD differs from the current one, the controller
          publishes a \texttt{modcod\_out} message.
    \item \textbf{BB Framer} receives the new MODCOD message on its
          \texttt{modcod\_in} port and applies it from the \emph{next frame}.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Orbit Visualizer} (\texttt{dvbs2acm\_orbit\_visualizer}): A
supplementary display block that receives two message connections ---
\texttt{channel\_state} from the LEO Channel and \texttt{modcod\_out} from
the ACM Controller.  It renders a real-time two-tab Qt GUI window showing a
polar sky-view plot (elevation vs azimuth) and a ground-track map, annotating
the satellite position with the current MODCOD and SNR at each instant.  This
block does not affect the signal chain or control loop; it is a monitoring
tool only.

The round-trip latency of the control loop is approximately 1--3 frames
(33--100~ms at 1~Msps), dominated by the ACM Feedback's 100~ms rate limiter.
This is fast enough for rain fade events (seconds) but not for burst noise.

\subsection{Tab 0 --- Control}

\textbf{SNR slider} (\texttt{qtgui\_range}, \texttt{snr\_db}): Controls the
noise amplitude of the AWGN channel in real time.  Range 20--50\,dB, step
0.5\,dB, default 33\,dB.  Because the signal chain has an intrinsic power
offset of $-16.6$\,dB, the measured SNR at the SNR Estimator ranges from
$\approx$\,3.4\,dB (\texttt{snr\_db}\,=\,20) to 33.4\,dB
(\texttt{snr\_db}\,=\,50).  The default of 33\,dB places the TCA measured
SNR at 16.4\,dB and the AOS/LOS SNR at 4.2\,dB, covering QPSK~1/4 through
16APSK~8/9 across one pass.  Use this slider to:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Manually trigger MODCOD switching without waiting for the LEO orbital arc
    \item Test the hysteresis of the rule-based controller by stepping the slider
    \item Observe PL Sync acquisition behaviour by reducing SNR toward the minimum
    \item Compare rule-based vs DQN reaction speed to step changes in noise
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Rain Rate slider} (\texttt{qtgui\_range}, \texttt{rain\_rate}):
Controls the ITU-R P.618-13 rain attenuation input to the LEO Channel block
in real time.  Range 0--50~mm/hr, step 1~mm/hr, default 5~mm/hr.  Moving
this slider simulates the onset of a rain fade event without restarting the
flowgraph.

\subsection{Tab 1 --- Constellations}

\textbf{TX Constellation Sink} (Modulator output): Shows the ideal
transmitted constellation with no noise. You should see:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{QPSK (IDs 1--11):} 4 points at $45^\circ, 135^\circ, 225^\circ, 315^\circ$ on a circle
    \item \textbf{8PSK (IDs 12--17):} 8 points equally spaced on a circle
    \item \textbf{16APSK (IDs 18--23):} Two rings (inner 4 points, outer 12)
    \item \textbf{32APSK (IDs 24--28):} Three rings (4 + 12 + 16 points)
\end{itemize}
Watch this panel change in real time as the ACM controller switches MODCODs.

\textbf{RX Constellation Sink} (PL Sync output): Shows received symbols after
carrier recovery and Gold descrambling.  In good channel conditions it matches
the TX constellation with scattered noise around each decision point (tighter
clusters = higher SNR).

\textbf{Diagnostic states:}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \emph{Ring/ellipse rotating}: PL Sync not yet locked; frequency
          offset not yet compensated.
    \item \emph{4 blobs, no clear structure}: PL Sync locked but SNR very low.
    \item \emph{Clean 4-point QPSK cluster}: Good SNR, carrier recovery working.
    \item \emph{8 points visible}: 8PSK mode active ($\text{SNR} \approx 7$--11~dB).
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Tab 2 --- Spectrum}

\textbf{Dual Frequency Sink}: A two-input frequency sink showing the spectrum
\emph{before} and \emph{after} the PL Sync block on the same axes:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Channel 0 (blue):} AWGN output --- raw received signal
          spectrum.  The DVB-S2 PL frame has a characteristic flat-top
          spectrum (Gold scrambling randomises the data).  Any Doppler offset
          shifts this flat-top left or right.
    \item \textbf{Channel 1 (red):} PL Sync output --- after frequency
          correction.  The flat-top should be centred at DC (0\,Hz).
\end{itemize}
If the two spectra are offset from each other, the PL Sync has not yet locked.
After lock, both should be centred and overlapping.

\subsection{Tab 3 --- Time / Waterfall}

\textbf{Time Sink} (AWGN output): Shows the amplitude of the received IQ
signal vs time.  Key observations:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Constant amplitude:} Good channel, no fading.
    \item \textbf{Slow amplitude variation:} Rician fading or range-change from the LEO orbit.
    \item \textbf{Rapid amplitude dips:} Rain attenuation or scintillation.
    \item \textbf{Sudden sharp drop:} Link loss / LOS (satellite below horizon).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Waterfall Sink} (AWGN output): A scrolling spectrogram (frequency on
x-axis, time flowing downward, colour = power level).  Useful for observing:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Doppler drift:} The flat-top DVB-S2 spectrum drifting
          horizontally over time as the satellite moves.
    \item \textbf{Modulation changes:} Subtle spectral density changes when
          MODCOD switches.
    \item \textbf{Interference:} Narrowband interference appears as bright
          vertical stripes.
\end{itemize}

\begin{keyinsight}
\textbf{What this chapter covers.}
Quantitative evidence that the system works across three channel scenarios:
a full SNR sweep exercising all 28 MODCODs, a physics-based LEO pass at 500~km
altitude, and a 10~dB rain fade event.  For each scenario the three strategies

% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Simulation Setup}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

All three scenarios use the same simulation framework:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Symbol rate}: 5~Msym/s (USRP B200 target).
  \item \textbf{Frame rate}: $5\times10^6 / 33282 \approx 150$ frames/s
        (QPSK 1/2 with pilots).
  \item \textbf{SNR estimator noise}: $\sigma_{\text{est}} = 0.3$~dB Gaussian
        jitter added to each raw SNR sample to model pilot-based estimation error.
  \item \textbf{LQI averaging}: 15-sample moving average ($\approx 1$~s at
        15~Hz ACM update rate) applied before MODCOD decisions, matching
        standard DVB-S2 ACM implementations~\cite{bischl2010}.
  \item \textbf{Hysteresis}: upgrade if SNR $\geq$ threshold $+1.5$~dB;
        downgrade if SNR $<$ threshold $+0.5$~dB (1.0~dB deadband),
        matching the production controller (Section~\ref{sec:acm_controller}).
  \item \textbf{Strategies compared}: CCM (fixed QPSK~1/2), rule-based ACM
        (threshold + hysteresis), DQN ACM (Dueling Double DQN, simplified
        16-dim state for simulation — the production ZMQ engine uses 52-dim,
        Section~\ref{ch:ai}; $\varepsilon = 0.05$ after convergence).
  \item \textbf{BER model}: DVB-S2 waterfall approximation
        (Table~\ref{tab:ber_model}).
\end{itemize}

\begin{table}[H]

\end{table}

The \textbf{QEF metric} (Quasi-Error-Free) is the fraction of frames with
BER $< 10^{-7}$, as defined by the DVB-S2 standard~\cite{etsi_dvbs2}.

\textbf{Switches} & \textbf{QEF (\%)} & \textbf{TEF} \\

The LEO pass scenario reveals the fundamental tension between spectral
efficiency and link stability on rapidly varying channels.

With 1-second LQI averaging, rule-based ACM switch count drops to 398 per
pass --- far more realistic --- and achieves $\bar{\eta} = 3.02$~bits/sym, a
$3\times$ gain over CCM.  However QEF = 64.3\%: the 15-sample average cannot
fully suppress the 12.2~dB SNR swing across the pass, and the rule-based
controller still reacts to averaged SNR values that include transient fading
dips, spending time below the QEF operating point.

The DQN agent achieves \textbf{QEF = 96.3\%} --- a \textbf{32 percentage point
improvement} over rule-based ACM --- while delivering $\bar{\eta} = 1.60$~bits/sym
(62\% gain over CCM).  The DQN's 16-element SNR history provides a richer
channel state than the instantaneous averaged LQI: it can distinguish a genuine
rising or falling trend from a momentary spike, allowing it to pre-position the
MODCOD rather than reacting after the fact.  The 858 DQN switches include
approximately 400 due to $\varepsilon = 0.05$ random exploration; a deployed
agent ($\varepsilon = 0$) would approach the rule-based switch count while
maintaining the QEF advantage.

\begin{keyinsight}
\textbf{SNR history enables proactive, not reactive, ACM.}
Rule-based ACM with LQI averaging sees a scalar averaged SNR; the DQN sees a
16-point history vector.  On a LEO pass the history reveals whether SNR is
rising (AOS to TCA) or falling (TCA to LOS).  The DQN learns to select a
higher MODCOD during the rising phase and drop down proactively during the
falling phase, achieving 32 percentage points better QEF at the cost of lower
peak $\eta$.  For satellite links where link drops are unrecoverable within a
pass, this reliability advantage is operationally critical.
\end{keyinsight}

\begin{keyinsight}
\textbf{Key finding: DQN delivers reliability without sacrificing TEF.}
On the rain fade scenario the DQN achieves the highest TEF (1.619) of all
three strategies, combining 99.3\% QEF with a 64\% spectral efficiency gain
over CCM.  On the LEO pass, the DQN achieves 96.3\% QEF (vs 64.3\% for
rule-based) while delivering a 56\% TEF gain over CCM.

The performance gap is driven by the DQN's temporal awareness: its 16-sample
SNR history allows proactive MODCOD pre-positioning before fades deepen,
which neither CCM nor the instantaneous-LQI rule-based controller can achieve.
This translates directly to better link availability in the operationally
constrained 9.2-min LEO pass window where frame losses are unrecoverable.
\end{keyinsight}

\section{ACM Controller Console Output Format}
\label{sec:console_output}
% -----------------------------------------------------------------------------

The ACM controller prints to the console \emph{only} when a MODCOD switch
occurs — there are no periodic heartbeat messages.  Each line uses the
\texttt{[GRC-ACM]} prefix followed by an up (\texttt{\^{}}) or down
(\texttt{v}) arrow indicating the direction of the switch:

\begin{lstlisting}[language=bash, caption={ACM controller console format: one line printed per MODCOD switch}]
[GRC-ACM] ^ QPSK 1/2 -> 16APSK 2/3  | SNR=+11.4 dB  el=45.2  Doppler=+52.3 kHz  RTT=4.1 ms
[GRC-ACM] ^ 16APSK 2/3 -> 32APSK 3/4| SNR=+16.9 dB  el=82.5  Doppler=+12.1 kHz  RTT=3.4 ms
[GRC-ACM] v 32APSK 3/4 -> 8PSK 3/4  | SNR=+7.8 dB   el=6.1   Doppler=-145.2 kHz RTT=12.8 ms
\end{lstlisting}

Each line shows:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
  \item \textbf{Direction}: \texttt{\^{}} = upgrade (higher-order modulation),
        \texttt{v} = downgrade (more robust modulation)
  \item \textbf{Transition}: old MODCOD name $\to$ new MODCOD name
  \item \textbf{Smoothed SNR}: exponentially averaged SNR at the time of switch
  \item \textbf{Orbital state}: elevation angle, Doppler shift, and RTT from
        the LEO channel model's \texttt{channel\_state} message
\end{itemize}

The absence of output between two switch lines confirms the hysteresis
deadband is working — the controller held the same MODCOD while SNR varied
within the $[\text{SNR}_\text{min} + 1.0,\;\text{SNR}_\text{min} + 2.0]$~dB
safe zone.

\chapter{Engineering Challenges and Solutions}
% =============================================================================

\begin{keyinsight}
\textbf{What this chapter covers.}
Six non-trivial engineering problems encountered during development, each with a
clear description of the root cause and the solution applied.  These include
the GNU Radio buffer size mismatch for large LDPC frames, Doppler-induced
constellation spinning, FLL block incompatibility at 1~sample/symbol, a GRC
YAML parser bug, Python module discovery by GRC, and message port flooding.
\end{keyinsight}

\section{GNU Radio Scheduler and Frame Size Mismatch}

\textbf{Problem}: DVB-S2 LDPC frames are 64,800 items long. GNU Radio's default
output buffer is approximately 8,192 items. When a block tries to write an
entire 64,800-item frame in one call but only 8,192 slots are available in the
output buffer, the excess items are lost (silent data corruption) or the block
deadlocks waiting for space.

\textbf{Solution}: Use \texttt{set\_output\_multiple(N)} in every streaming
block's \texttt{\_\_init\_\_}. This tells the GNU Radio scheduler to never call
\texttt{general\_work} until at least $N$ output slots are free. The block can
then always write one complete frame per \texttt{general\_work} call with no
risk of overflow or partial writes.

\section{Spinning Constellation (Doppler Frequency Offset)}

\textbf{Problem}: When the carrier frequency offset is non-zero, the received
IQ constellation rotates continuously. Every received sample is rotated by
$2\pi f_d / f_s$ more than the previous. The constellation traces rings or
ellipses rather than sitting at discrete points, making symbol detection
impossible.

\textbf{Root Cause in Original Implementation}: The original PL Sync used the
\emph{magnitude} of the SOF correlation to detect frame starts and discarded
the \emph{phase}. Without phase information, frequency offset could not be
estimated, only static phase could be corrected.

\textbf{Solution}: The SOF correlation returns a complex value. Its phase is
the carrier phase at the SOF location. The frequency offset is estimated from
the phase difference between consecutive SOF correlations:
$\hat{f} = \Delta\phi / \Delta t$. This estimate drives a slow feedback loop
($\alpha_f = 0.05$) to gradually correct the frequency. Additionally, pilot
blocks provide dense phase corrections every 1476 symbols via a pilot PLL.

\section{FLL Band-Edge Filter at 1 Sample/Symbol}

\textbf{Problem}: The GNU Radio \texttt{digital\_fll\_band\_edge\_cc} block
requires a signal oversampled at $\geq 2$ samples/symbol with RRC (Root-Raised
Cosine) pulse shaping applied. Without RRC filtering, the band-edge regions
contain no signal energy and the FLL error signal is pure noise. Connecting the
FLL to a 1 sps DVB-S2 signal made frequency recovery worse, not better.

\textbf{Solution}: Disable the FLL in the flowgraph (\texttt{state: disabled})
and rely entirely on the SOF-based inter-frame frequency estimator and
pilot-aided PLL inside the PL Sync block. These work correctly at 1 sps
because they use the known structure of the DVB-S2 frame (SOF sequence and
pilot symbols) rather than signal band edges.

\section{GRC YAML Folded Scalar Bug}

\textbf{Problem}: GRC block descriptor files use YAML for parameter
definitions. The \texttt{options} key for enum parameters was written using
the YAML ``folded scalar'' syntax (\texttt{options: >}), which collapses
multi-line text into a single string. GRC expects a list (array), not a string,
and fails to open the block properties dialog when it receives a string.

\textbf{Solution}: Change all \texttt{options: >} folded scalars to inline YAML
lists:
\begin{lstlisting}[language=bash, caption={Correct YAML list syntax for GRC enum options}]
# WRONG: folded scalar produces a string, not a list
options: >
  Hybrid
  Pilot-MMSE
  Blind-M2M4

# CORRECT: inline list
options: ["Hybrid", "Pilot-MMSE", "Blind-M2M4"]
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Python Module Discovery by GRC}

\textbf{Problem}: GNU Radio Companion (GRC) launches Python without sourcing
\texttt{\textasciitilde/.bashrc}, so the \texttt{PYTHONPATH} environment
variable is not set. Python 3.12 automatically discovers packages only in
\texttt{python3.12/site-packages/}, not in the generic
\texttt{python3/dist-packages/} path that was previously used.

\textbf{Solution}: The \texttt{build.sh} script installs the Python module to
both paths simultaneously, using Python's own version detection to construct
the correct versioned path:
\begin{lstlisting}[language=bash, caption={Dual-path Python installation in build.sh}]
PY_VER=$(python3 -c "import sys; print(f'{sys.version_info.major}.{sys.version_info.minor}')")
INSTALL_PY="${HOME}/.local/lib/python3/dist-packages/dvbs2acm"
INSTALL_PY_VER="${HOME}/.local/lib/python${PY_VER}/site-packages/dvbs2acm"
mkdir -p "${INSTALL_PY}" "${INSTALL_PY_VER}"
cp python/dvbs2acm/*.py "${INSTALL_PY}/"
cp python/dvbs2acm/*.py "${INSTALL_PY_VER}/"
\end{lstlisting}

\section{Message Port Flooding}

\textbf{Problem}: The ACM controller published a \texttt{modcod\_out} message on
every single feedback report (approximately 100 per second). This flooded the
GNU Radio message bus, caused the console to scroll unreadably fast, and
introduced measurable scheduling overhead.

\textbf{Solution}: Three changes:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Track the previous MODCOD and only publish when it \emph{changes}.
    \item Rate-limit statistics (\texttt{stats\_out}) to at most 1~Hz using
          \texttt{time.time()} to timestamp the last publish.
    \item The ACM Feedback block aggregates SNR reports and sends to the
          controller at most 10~Hz, not once per frame.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Summary of Contributions}

This project has implemented a complete DVB-S2 Adaptive Coding and Modulation
system as a GNU Radio Out-of-Tree module, targeting LEO satellite communications
at X-Band. The key contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{10 GNU Radio blocks} implementing the full DVB-S2 ACM TX/RX
          chain in pure Python, without requiring C++ compilation:
          BB Framer, FEC Encoder, Modulator, PL Framer, PL Sync, SNR Estimator,
          Demodulator, FEC Decoder, ACM Feedback, and ACM Controller.

    \item \textbf{Full carrier recovery} in the PL Sync block: per-sample
          frequency+phase correction, inter-frame frequency estimation from
          SOF phase differences, pilot-aided PLL for residual phase tracking,
          and Müller \& Mueller timing error detection.

    \item \textbf{Encoder-guided LDPC + BCH FEC decoding}: iterative
          bit-flipping LDPC decoder using gr-dtv as a syndrome oracle, providing
          0.5--0.8~dB coding gain over hard-decision. BCH outer decoder with
          single-bit correction capability.

    \item \textbf{ITU-R-compliant LEO channel model}: orbital mechanics, FSPL,
          Doppler shift, and a full six-layer atmospheric impairment stack
          per ITU-R P.618-13~\cite{itu_p618}, P.838-3~\cite{itu_p838},
          P.839-4~\cite{itu_p839}, P.676-12~\cite{itu_p676},
          P.840-8~\cite{itu_p840}, and P.681-11~\cite{itu_p681}, with
          time-correlated AR(1) fading consistent with 3GPP
          TR~38.811~\cite{3gpp_tr38811} NTN channel parameters.

    \item \textbf{AI/ML cognitive ACM engine}: Double DQN with 52-dimensional
          state vector (16 SNR history + 5 LEO orbital geometry features +
          28 MODCOD one-hot + 3 link quality scalars), CNN+LSTM channel
          predictor, and online training via Prioritized Experience Replay.
          Offline pre-training on a sweep scenario ($-3$ to $16$\,dB) produces
          a model that immediately covers the full MODCOD range when deployed
          in GRC.  The converged DQN achieves 96--99\% QEF at 1.6--1.8~bits/sym,
          versus 47--73\% QEF at 2.1--3.0~bits/sym for rule-based ACM.
          The DQN trades peak spectral efficiency for link reliability, delivering
          a 56--64\% TEF gain over CCM on dynamic LEO and rain-fade channels.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Current Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{FEC decoder speed}: Each guided LDPC iteration requires one
          gr-dtv encoder call ($\sim$20--50~ms). At 3 iterations, decoding takes
          60--150~ms per frame, making real-time operation impossible at full
          DVB-S2 frame rates. The system currently operates in simulation/test mode.
    \item \textbf{Timing correction}: The M\&M TED estimates timing error but
          cannot correct it at 1 sps. Actual timing correction requires
          $\geq 2$ sps and a polyphase resampling filter.
    \item \textbf{DQN online convergence}: Although offline pre-training
          provides a good initial policy, the online training thread continues
          to refine the model during GRC operation. Performance improves
          progressively across multiple passes as the replay buffer fills with
          real channel transitions.
\end{itemize}

\chapter{Glossary of Terms and Acronyms}
% =============================================================================

\noindent
The following terms and acronyms appear throughout the report.  Each entry
gives a brief definition in plain language.

\begin{longtable}{p{3.2cm}p{10.5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Term / Acronym} & \textbf{Definition} \\
\midrule
\endhead
\bottomrule
\endfoot

\textbf{ACM} & \textit{Adaptive Coding and Modulation.}  The ability to
    change both the constellation (modulation order) and the FEC code rate
    on every frame to match the current channel quality. \\[4pt]

\textbf{APSK} & \textit{Amplitude and Phase Shift Keying.}  A constellation
    that places points on multiple concentric rings of different radii (unlike
    PSK, which uses a single ring).  16APSK has two rings; 32APSK has three. \\[4pt]

\textbf{AOS} & \textit{Acquisition of Signal.}  The moment a LEO satellite
    rises above the horizon mask (typically $5^\circ$ elevation) and the link
    becomes active. \\[4pt]

\textbf{AWGN} & \textit{Additive White Gaussian Noise.}  A standard noise
    model: thermal noise with a flat spectrum added to every sample.  ``White''
    means equal power at all frequencies; ``Gaussian'' means sample values follow
    a bell-curve distribution. \\[4pt]

\textbf{BCH} & \textit{Bose--Chaudhuri--Hocquenghem code.}  An outer algebraic
    error-correction code used in DVB-S2.  It can correct up to $t$ bit errors
    per codeword (typically $t = 8$ or $12$) after the LDPC decoder. \\[4pt]

\textbf{BER} & \textit{Bit Error Rate.}  The fraction of decoded bits that are
    incorrect.  DVB-S2's target is BER\,$< 10^{-7}$ (quasi-error-free). \\[4pt]

\textbf{CCM} & \textit{Constant Coding and Modulation.}  Operating at a fixed
    MODCOD regardless of channel conditions.  Simple but inefficient when the
    channel varies. \\[4pt]

\textbf{DQN} & \textit{Deep Q-Network.}  A reinforcement learning algorithm
    that uses a neural network to approximate the Q-value function, enabling
    decision-making in high-dimensional, continuous state spaces. \\[4pt]

\textbf{DVB-S2} & \textit{Digital Video Broadcasting -- Satellite, Second
    Generation.}  The international standard for satellite broadband transmission,
    defined by ETSI EN~302~307-1.  Supports QPSK, 8PSK, 16APSK, and 32APSK
    with 11 code rates. \\[4pt]

\textbf{ETSI} & \textit{European Telecommunications Standards Institute.}  The
    standards body that publishes the DVB-S2 specification. \\[4pt]

\textbf{FEC} & \textit{Forward Error Correction.}  A technique that adds
    redundant bits (parity) to the transmitted data so the receiver can correct
    errors without retransmission. \\[4pt]

\textbf{FER} & \textit{Frame Error Rate.}  The fraction of received frames that
    contain uncorrected errors after FEC decoding. \\[4pt]

\textbf{FSPL} & \textit{Free-Space Path Loss.}  The attenuation of a radio
    signal due to geometric spreading as it propagates through free space.
    Increases as $20\log_{10}(d)$ dB with distance $d$. \\[4pt]

\textbf{Gold code} & A pseudo-random binary sequence with excellent
    autocorrelation properties, used in DVB-S2 to scramble the Physical Layer
    frame and suppress spectral lines. \\[4pt]

\textbf{GRC} & \textit{GNU Radio Companion.}  The graphical drag-and-drop
    editor for building GNU Radio flowgraphs. \\[4pt]

\textbf{IQ / I/Q} & \textit{In-phase and Quadrature.}  The two orthogonal
    components of a complex baseband signal, corresponding to the real and
    imaginary parts.  Together they describe the amplitude and phase of the
    signal at any instant. \\[4pt]

\textbf{LDPC} & \textit{Low-Density Parity-Check code.}  The primary
    error-correction code in DVB-S2.  Operates very close to the Shannon
    theoretical limit.  DVB-S2 normal frames use a 64,800-bit LDPC codeword. \\[4pt]

\textbf{LEO} & \textit{Low Earth Orbit.}  Orbit at altitude below $\sim$2000~km.
    This project targets 500~km circular orbit, giving a pass duration of
    $\sim$9.2 minutes and orbital velocity of 7.62~km/s. \\[4pt]

\textbf{LLR} & \textit{Log-Likelihood Ratio.}  A real number representing how
    confident the receiver is about a decoded bit.  Large positive = confident
    zero; large negative = confident one; near zero = uncertain. \\[4pt]

\textbf{LOS} & \textit{Loss of Signal.}  The moment a LEO satellite sets below
    the horizon mask and the link is lost. \\[4pt]

\textbf{LSTM} & \textit{Long Short-Term Memory.}  A type of recurrent neural
    network capable of learning long-range temporal dependencies.  Used here
    in the CNN+LSTM SNR predictor. \\[4pt]

\textbf{M2M4} & The second/fourth-moment blind SNR estimator.  Estimates SNR
    from the statistical moments of the received signal without knowing the
    transmitted symbols. \\[4pt]

\textbf{MDP} & \textit{Markov Decision Process.}  A mathematical framework
    for sequential decision-making.  Defined by state space, action space,
    transition probabilities, and reward function. \\[4pt]

\textbf{MODCOD} & \textit{Modulation--Coding scheme.}  A specific combination
    of constellation and FEC code rate.  DVB-S2 defines 28 MODCODs for normal
    frames, numbered 1--28. \\[4pt]

\textbf{OOT module} & \textit{Out-of-Tree module.}  A set of custom GNU Radio
    blocks distributed separately from the main GNU Radio installation. \\[4pt]

\textbf{PER} & \textit{Prioritized Experience Replay.}  A DQN training technique
    that samples transitions from the replay buffer in proportion to their
    learning value (TD error), rather than uniformly at random. \\[4pt]

\textbf{PL frame} & \textit{Physical Layer frame.}  The top-level transmission
    unit in DVB-S2: PLHEADER (90 symbols) + data slots + optional pilot blocks. \\[4pt]

\textbf{PLHEADER} & The 90-symbol header at the start of every DVB-S2 PL frame,
    comprising the 26-symbol SOF and the 64-symbol PLSCODE. \\[4pt]

\textbf{PLSCODE} & The 64 BPSK symbols that encode the 7-bit MODCOD/pilots
    header.  Uses a (64,7) Reed-Muller code with minimum Hamming distance 32,
    readable down to $E_b/N_0 \approx -2.4$~dB. \\[4pt]

\textbf{PLL} & \textit{Phase-Locked Loop.}  A feedback control loop that
    continuously corrects the receiver's phase estimate to track the transmitter's
    carrier phase, compensating for oscillator drift and Doppler-induced phase
    rotation. \\[4pt]

\textbf{PMT} & \textit{Polymorphic Type.}  GNU Radio's generic message type,
    used for passing dictionaries, integers, booleans, and other data through
    message ports between blocks. \\[4pt]

\textbf{PL Sync} & \textit{Physical Layer Synchroniser.}  The receiver block
    that detects frame boundaries (SOF correlator), corrects carrier frequency
    offset, tracks phase with a PLL, Gold-descrambles the frame, and decodes
    the PLSCODE. \\[4pt]

\textbf{PSK} & \textit{Phase Shift Keying.}  A modulation family where bits
    are encoded as phase shifts of a carrier.  QPSK uses 4 phases; 8PSK uses 8. \\[4pt]

\textbf{QPSK} & \textit{Quadrature Phase Shift Keying.}  4-point constellation
    with phases at $45^\circ$, $135^\circ$, $225^\circ$, $315^\circ$.
    Most robust DVB-S2 modulation; 2 bits per symbol. \\[4pt]

\textbf{QEF} & \textit{Quasi-Error-Free.}  The DVB-S2 operating point at which
    BER\,$< 10^{-7}$ after the BCH outer decoder. \\[4pt]

\textbf{RL} & \textit{Reinforcement Learning.}  A machine learning paradigm
    where an agent learns a policy by trial and error, using reward signals rather
    than labelled training data. \\[4pt]

\textbf{RTT} & \textit{Round-Trip Time.}  The time for a signal to travel
    from the transmitter to the receiver and back.  On a 500~km LEO link:
    3.3~ms at zenith to 13.6~ms at the horizon. \\[4pt]

\textbf{SNR} & \textit{Signal-to-Noise Ratio.}  The ratio of signal power to
    noise power, usually expressed in decibels.  The primary metric used by
    the ACM controller to select the MODCOD. \\[4pt]

\textbf{SOF} & \textit{Start of Frame.}  The fixed 26-symbol BPSK sequence at
    the beginning of every DVB-S2 PLHEADER, used for frame synchronisation. \\[4pt]

\textbf{Stream tag} & A GNU Radio metadata label attached to a specific sample
    in a data stream, travelling automatically with the data through all
    downstream blocks.  This project uses \texttt{"modcod"}, \texttt{"frame\_start"},
    and \texttt{"pilots"} tags. \\[4pt]

\textbf{TCA} & \textit{True/Time of Closest Approach.}  The moment in a LEO
    pass when the satellite is at its highest elevation angle (closest to directly
    overhead), corresponding to minimum slant range and maximum SNR. \\[4pt]

\textbf{TD error} & \textit{Temporal Difference error.}  The difference between
    the Q-value predicted by the neural network and the Bellman-target Q-value
    computed from the observed reward and next state.  Large TD error means the
    network is wrong and should learn from this transition. \\[4pt]

\textbf{VCM} & \textit{Variable Coding and Modulation.}  A mode where MODCOD
    changes are pre-scheduled (not feedback-driven).  Less adaptive than ACM. \\[4pt]

\textbf{ZMQ} & \textit{ZeroMQ.}  A high-performance asynchronous messaging
    library.  Used here for the REQ/REP socket between the GNU Radio ACM
    Controller block and the AI engine Python process. \\[4pt]

\end{longtable}
